# Tasar — Crossroads of Civilization

**Eren Tasar, *Crossroads of Civilization: A History of Central Asia* (The Great Courses, 2025)**
24 lectures, ~11h15m. Narrator: Tasar. PDF study guide included.

Tasar is a historian at UNC Chapel Hill specializing in Soviet Central Asia and Islam under Soviet rule. His scholarly monograph *Soviet and Muslim: The Institutionalization of Islam in Central Asia* (Oxford, 2017) is the academic complement to this survey course.

---

## What it argues

A comprehensive survey of Central Asian history from the Scythians through modern nation-states. Tasar's emphasis is on Central Asia as a place that *generated* civilization — not a blank space between China and Europe but the crossroads where ideas, technologies, and religions mixed and were transmitted. The lectures cover nomadic cultures, the Achaemenid/Arab/Mongol imperial layers, the Samanid-Timurid-Shibanid cultural achievements, Tsarist colonialism, Soviet rule, and the post-Soviet states.

---

## What we use it for

- **Orienting source.** This is what seeded the project. Dave's geographic-determinist framework collided with Tasar's material during a walk in Gainesville, FL.
- **Chapter 4 (Silk Road):** Tasar's treatment of the corridor as a generative space, not just a trade route, supports our argument about the Silk Road as circulatory system.
- **Chapter 6 (Parallel Conquests):** Tasar covers Tsarist colonial policies and the cotton economy — the administrative and economic aftermath of the railroad conquest. His perspective from the Central Asian side complements Morrison's military/imperial focus.
- **Chapter 7 (Timurid Paradox):** Tasar covers the Timurid cultural flowering in the lecture format — useful as orientation before going deeper with Starr.
- **General:** Tasar's framing of Central Asia as a crossroads (the title itself) is the geographic thesis stated as a historian's organizing principle without the probabilistic language we add.

## Limitations

- Lecture format means survey depth — no single topic gets the treatment a monograph provides. For the Russian conquest, Morrison goes much deeper. For the intellectual golden age, Starr goes much deeper.
- Tasar's expertise is strongest in the Soviet period. The earlier material is competent synthesis, not original research.
- The Great Courses format is pedagogical — it explains rather than argues. We need sources that argue.
