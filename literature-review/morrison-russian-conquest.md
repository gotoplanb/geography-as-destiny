# Morrison — The Russian Conquest of Central Asia

**Alexander Morrison, *The Russian Conquest of Central Asia: A Study in Imperial Expansion, 1814–1914* (Cambridge University Press, 2021)**

Morrison is at the University of Oxford, one of the leading Western scholars of Russian imperial history in Central Asia. This is the definitive modern English-language scholarly treatment of the conquest.

---

## What it argues

Morrison dismantles the "Great Game" narrative — the idea that the Russian conquest was a coherent strategic campaign driven by rivalry with Britain. Instead, he shows the conquest was often ad hoc, driven by local commanders exceeding their orders, bureaucratic momentum, frontier security logic, and the opportunities that new military technology (particularly the railroad) created. St. Petersburg frequently learned about conquests after the fact. The result was the same — total incorporation of the steppe and oasis cities — but the *process* was messier and more contingent than traditional narratives suggest.

---

## What we use it for

- **Chapter 6 (Parallel Conquests) — SPINE SOURCE for the Russian side.** Morrison provides the detailed military chronology: Tashkent (1865), Bukhara (1868), Khiva (1873), Geok Tepe (1881), Merv (1884). Each campaign has specific geographic and logistic detail we need.
- **The friction-collapse argument:** Morrison's account of the Transcaspian Railway (1880–1888) documents exactly how the railroad transformed the military calculus. Before rail: campaigns into the steppe were logistically agonizing, limited by water and supply lines. After rail: reinforcement, supply, and administrative control became trivial.
- **The "ad hoc" argument strengthens our thesis:** If the conquest wasn't centrally planned but produced the same structural outcome as the American expansion (which was *also* partly ad hoc — see White), that's *more* evidence for geographic causation, not less. The individuals were improvising. The terrain + technology dictated the outcome range.
- **Anti-great-man:** Morrison's emphasis on structural and institutional drivers over individual genius directly supports our framework.

## Limitations

- Morrison is focused on the Russian imperial perspective and archival sources. The Central Asian experience of being conquered is thinner here — Tasar, Eden, and Brower complement from that side.
- The book is dense, scholarly, 600+ pages. It argues with other historians as much as it narrates events. Rewarding but not a fast read.
- Morrison stops at 1914. The Soviet transformation of Central Asia is outside his scope.
