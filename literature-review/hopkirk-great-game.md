# Hopkirk — The Great Game

**Peter Hopkirk, *The Great Game: The Struggle for Empire in Central Asia* (Kodansha International, 1992)**

Hopkirk was a journalist and travel writer, not an academic historian. This is popular narrative history — well-researched, vividly written, widely read.

---

## What it argues

The Anglo-Russian rivalry in Central Asia during the 19th century was a sustained strategic contest ("the Great Game") for influence over the region between India and Russia. Hopkirk narrates this through the adventures of individual officers, spies, and explorers on both sides — the British pushing north from India, the Russians pushing south from Siberia, with Central Asia as the contested middle ground.

---

## What we use it for

- **Chapter 6 (Parallel Conquests) — narrative texture.** Hopkirk writes well about terrain, weather, the experience of campaigning in the steppe and desert. His account of Geok Tepe and Skobelev's assault is the best available in English for the ground-level human cost.
- **The Turkmen resistance:** Hopkirk gives the Turkmen perspective more color than Morrison's archival approach allows.
- **General readability model:** Hopkirk demonstrates that Central Asian history can be told in a way that grips a general reader. Our narrative descent sections should aim for at least this level of vividness.

## Limitations

- **Morrison's core critique applies:** Hopkirk buys the "Great Game" framing too uncritically. He treats the conquest as primarily a strategic chess match between London and St. Petersburg, which centers European agency and downplays the geographic/structural drivers we argue for.
- Popular history's bias toward individual actors — Hopkirk loves the colorful officer, the daring spy. This is great man history wearing adventure clothes. We use him for texture, not analysis.
- Published 1992 — pre-dates the archival revolution in Russian imperial history that Morrison benefits from.
