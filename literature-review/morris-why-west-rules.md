# Morris — Why the West Rules — For Now

**Ian Morris, *Why the West Rules — For Now: The Patterns of History, and What They Reveal About the Future* (Farrar, Straus and Giroux, 2010)**

Morris is a historian and archaeologist at Stanford. This book builds a quantitative framework for comparing Eastern and Western civilizational development over 15,000 years.

---

## What it argues

Morris constructs a "social development index" — a composite score measuring energy capture, social organization, war-making capacity, and information technology — and tracks it for both Eastern and Western civilizations from the Ice Age to the present. His conclusion: "maps, not chaps." Geography explains the broad pattern of which region led at which time. Individual leaders explain only the noise around that trend.

The critical addition to Diamond: Morris shows that geographic advantage *shifts* over time. The West doesn't always lead. The East led for long stretches. The advantage shifts because of what Morris calls "the paradox of development" — success in one era creates the conditions that undermine advantage in the next. This is closer to our friction-collapse model than Diamond's static geography.

---

## What we use it for

- **Chapter 1 (The Distribution) — the strongest existing version of our probabilistic frame.** "Maps, not chaps" is our thesis stated in four words. Morris's quantitative approach — imperfect as it is — demonstrates that you can apply statistical thinking to civilizational history.
- **The friction-collapse argument:** Morris's "paradox of development" is a version of punctuated equilibrium. He shows that geographic advantage isn't permanent — it shifts when technological or organizational changes alter what terrain matters. This isn't identical to our friction-collapse concept (Morris is gradual; we argue for sudden phase transitions) but it's the closest existing framework.
- **The quantitative question:** Morris shows both the promise and limits of trying to quantify geographic advantage. If Dave decides we want to include quantitative elements, Morris is the model and the cautionary tale.

## Limitations

- The social development index is debatable. The choice of what to measure, how to weight it, and how to score pre-literate societies involves judgment calls that critics have challenged. Morris is transparent about this, which is to his credit.
- Morris frames his argument as "West vs. East," which is itself a geographic simplification. Central Asia — the corridor between his two poles — barely appears. Our book lives in his blind spot.
- The "paradox of development" is more descriptive than mechanistic. He shows *that* advantage shifts; he doesn't fully explain *how* or *why* at the level of specific technology-geography interactions. That's what our friction-collapse concept adds.
