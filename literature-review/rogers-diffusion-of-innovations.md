# Rogers — Diffusion of Innovations

**Everett Rogers, *Diffusion of Innovations* (5th edition, Free Press, 2003; first published 1962)**

Rogers was a communication studies professor at the University of New Mexico. This book, revised through five editions over 40 years, is the foundational text on how innovations spread through populations. It has been cited over 150,000 times.

---

## What it argues

Innovations spread through populations in a predictable S-curve pattern. The rate of adoption depends on five perceived attributes: relative advantage, compatibility, complexity, trialability, and observability. Adopters fall into five categories: innovators (2.5%), early adopters (13.5%), early majority (34%), late majority (34%), and laggards (16%). The critical mass / tipping point occurs when early adopters demonstrate success to the early majority.

Rogers developed this empirically — from agricultural extension studies (how Iowa farmers adopted hybrid corn), not from theory. The statistical texture is baked into the framework from its origin.

---

## What we use it for

- **The third lens, applied throughout.** Rogers gives us the analytical toolkit for every chapter's diffusion analysis section. When we ask "how did this technology/idea/practice actually spread?", Rogers provides the vocabulary and the expected curve shape.
- **Chapter 1 (The Distribution):** Introduce Rogers clearly enough that the framework can be deployed without re-explanation in every subsequent chapter.
- **Chapter 5 (Islamic Diffusion):** The strongest direct application — Islam's two diffusion curves (conquest vs. trade routes) mapped onto Rogers' categories.
- **Chapter 6 (Parallel Conquests):** The railroad as a diffusion mechanism for state power. The S-curve of territorial control following rail lines.
- **Chapter 8 (Synthesis Frontier):** History's position at the laggard end of AI adoption. The prologue previews this; Chapter 8 develops it formally.
- **The meta-argument:** Rogers explains *why* this book will face resistance from historians and *when* that resistance will dissolve. The framework predicts its own reception.

## Limitations

- Rogers developed the framework for modern communication and agricultural contexts. Applying it to pre-modern historical diffusion requires care — we're extending the framework beyond its validated domain. The argument that it *should* apply (because diffusion dynamics are structural, not culturally specific) is part of our thesis, not a given.
- The adopter categories are statistical abstractions with specific percentage allocations (2.5%, 13.5%, etc.) based on normal distribution assumptions. In historical cases, we usually can't measure the actual percentages. We use the categories qualitatively, not quantitatively.
- Rogers is descriptive, not causal. He tells you *that* innovations spread in S-curves but doesn't deeply explain *why* at the level of individual decision-making. The geographic lens adds the "why" — adoption curves look the way they do because geography shapes who encounters the innovation first, who can try it cheaply, and who faces the highest switching cost.
