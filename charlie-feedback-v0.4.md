## New Issues

### #14 — Chapter 2 makes quantitative predictions it cannot yet cash

Ch. 2, Section VI proposes testing the settlement-gradient model against SRTM data and archaeological databases. This is excellent in principle — falsifiable, testable, requires no fieldwork. But it's presented as a prediction the reader can check, and the text never reports whether *you* checked it.

Did you run this test? If so, report the result. If not, acknowledge that it remains untested and explain why (e.g., the archaeological database work is beyond this project's scope). A prediction you could have tested but didn't is weaker than one you tested and confirmed. It looks like you're avoiding the risk of disconfirmation.

The prediction itself is strong — the asymmetric boundary signature (hard upstream, soft downstream) is a genuinely novel prediction. But the chapter ends on "the prediction follows" without resolving whether the prediction holds. That's a promissory note. Either cash it or acknowledge the debt.

### #15 — The "45 million deaths" claim needs sourcing

Ch. 4, Section VI: "Forty-five million deaths during the seventh- and eighth-century collapse is the cost of centuries of successful friction collapse meeting the constraint's reassertion."

This is an extraordinary claim. Where does the number come from? What event specifically? If this refers to the An Lushan Rebellion death toll (which is the commonly cited ~36 million figure, itself disputed), say so and cite the source. If it's a different calculation, explain it. A number this large, dropped without citation in a chapter whose endnotes say "to be added after review," is exactly the kind of thing that looks like pattern-matching to a dramatic figure rather than verified evidence. This is an evidence-strength risk.

### #16 — Chapter 3 has no counter-case

Chapters 1, 2, and 4 all include explicit counter-cases or failure modes. Ch. 3 does not. The Turkmenistan section (peace through irrelevance) is the *negative* prediction, not a counter-case. A counter-case would be: a place that looks like a chokepoint but doesn't behave like one — or a non-chokepoint that develops chokepoint characteristics. Without this, the chapter is unfalsifiable within its own frame. Every contested place is explained by chokepoint logic; every uncontested place is explained by irrelevance. Where does the model fail?

Candidate: Panama before the canal. Not a natural chokepoint (the natural route was around Cape Horn). It became one through engineering — which raises the question of whether human-engineered chokepoints behave the same as geographic ones, and whether the framework handles the distinction.

### #17 — Chapters 2–5 have no endnotes

The endnotes for Ch. 1 and Ch. 8 are the strongest part of the scholarly apparatus. They demonstrate intellectual lineage, cite reference cards, and show the thinking trail. Chapters 2–5 all end with "Endnotes to be added after review."

I understand the workflow — these are drafts awaiting your review pass. But I cannot fully evaluate the evidentiary basis of four chapters without their citations. For this review, I'm taking the arguments at face value. For the next review, I need the notes. The framework claims in these chapters (Saharan pump timing, Khazar Khaganate characterization, Richthofen's railroad motivation, Sogdian identity dissolution) are all verifiable claims that need sourcing before I can sign off.

### #18 — The Manichaeism argument is strong but needs one more move

Ch. 5's controlled-experiment framing is the best use of the diffusion lens in the book so far. The boundary-as-load-bearing-feature argument is genuinely original. But it needs one additional piece to be fully defensible:

**The timing objection.** Islam appeared 300 years after Manichaeism. By the time Islam was competing for the corridor, the geopolitical conditions had changed — the Sassanid and Roman empires that persecuted Manichaeism were different entities from the Abbasid and Byzantine empires that hosted Islam. A skeptical reader will ask: "Are you really holding geography constant? Or did the political substrate change in ways that advantaged Islam for reasons other than boundary strategy?"

The fix is simple: acknowledge the timing gap and argue that the boundary-strategy variable is *more* explanatory than the political-context variable because Islam also succeeded in contexts where Manichaeism also operated (e.g., the Uyghur sphere, where Manichaeism briefly succeeded precisely because of political patronage, then collapsed when the patronage ended — confirming that without boundary-drawing capacity, the religion couldn't survive withdrawal of state support).

You're 80% of the way there. The Uyghur example is already in the text. Just make the timing-objection explicit and argue through it.

## Structural Observations (Not Issues)

**The TOC restructuring works.** Part II (River and Wall) → Part III (Corridor) → Part IV (Collapse) tells a story of increasing friction collapse across history. The reader experiences the terrain holding, then shifting, then breaking. Good narrative architecture.

**Ch. 5 earns its place.** The Manichaeism contrast case is the strongest demonstration that this isn't just cherry-picking confirming examples. You have a *failure* predicted by the framework — maximum-entropy religion fails because friction zones are load-bearing. That's falsifiability in action: the framework predicts both success conditions and failure conditions.

**The Prologue is ready.** I had concerns about the AI-method section feeling defensive in v0.1. It no longer does. The canal/river framing in Ch. 8 retroactively strengthens the Prologue's claim. The two chapters bracket the book's method argument well.

**Ch. 4's friction-filter principle is the book's most testable claim.** The prediction that you can reconstruct route friction from archaeological content signatures — religious artifacts on high-friction routes, commercial records on low-friction routes — is elegant, falsifiable, and (to my knowledge) novel. If this holds across corridors beyond the Silk Road, it's a genuine contribution to historical methodology.
