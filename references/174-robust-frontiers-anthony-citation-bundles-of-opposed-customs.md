## What "Robust Frontier" Adds to "Persistent Frontier"

The two terms are related but not identical. "Persistent" describes the temporal property — the frontier persists across millennia. "Robust" describes the *mechanism* of persistence — the frontier is defined by *bundles of opposed customs* that co-occur because the terrain on each side selects for different adaptations simultaneously.

Three properties make a frontier robust in Anthony's sense:

1. **Multiple co-occurring distinctions.** Linguistic family differences. Material culture differences (pottery styles, burial practices, settlement patterns, weapon types, tool forms). Kinship structure differences. Subsistence economy differences. The frontier is not a single boundary — it is a bundle of boundaries that line up at the same geographic transition zone.

2. **Selection rather than imposition.** The bundle does not exist because someone drew lines or built walls. It exists because the terrain on each side rewards different specific adaptations. Steppe terrain rewards mobile pastoralism with horse-enabled extensive operations; farming terrain rewards fixed settlements with intensive agricultural cycles. Each side's bundle is a coherent adaptive response to its terrain. The contrast between bundles is the frontier.

3. **Continuous regeneration.** The bundle persists not because anyone maintains it but because the terrain keeps selecting for the same adaptations generation after generation. Populations can mix at the boundary. Individuals can cross. But the bundles regenerate because the underlying selection pressures regenerate. This is why frontiers persist across millennia: the terrain doesn't change.

## How This Specifies the Framework's Existing Cases

The "bundles of opposed customs" mechanism explains why specific dissertation cases produced the outcomes they did:

**The Comanche-settler frontier** (Chapter 6) was intractable because two completely different bundles of opposed customs met at a zone where neither terrain type clearly dominated. The Comanche bundle: horse culture, mobile pastoralism, raid-based status economy, kinship-based political organization, decentralized confederation, captive-incorporation mechanism, lingua franca commerce. The settler bundle: sedentary agriculture, fixed property, written contract law, centralized state administration, exclusive ethnic identity, monolingual encoding. Neither bundle could absorb the other. The terrain was not clearly enough of one type to force one bundle to displace the other. The frontier remained robust until industrial-scale extraction (cards 156, 157) collapsed the resource base that sustained the Comanche bundle.

**Sykes-Picot's failure** (Chapter 3, card 117) is the framework's clearest case of administrative lines failing against robust frontiers. The Zagros-Arabian desert corridor's bundle of opposed customs — Persian vs Arab linguistic substrates, mountain pastoralism vs desert nomadism vs riverine agriculture, Shia vs Sunni religious encoding, multiple millennia of ethnic distribution patterns — predated the 1916 border-drawing by five thousand years. The administrative line could not dissolve the bundle. The bundle kept reasserting itself as Iran-Iraq tensions, Kurdish identity persistence, Sunni-Shia conflict, and the specific contemporary patterns the framework reads as robust-frontier expressions.

**The four khanates** (Chapter 7, card 120) fragmented along robust frontier lines after the Mongol Empire's unified phase. The Yuan, Ilkhanate, Golden Horde, and Chagatai khanates each settled into a terrain context with its own bundle of opposed customs (Chinese, Persian, steppe-nomadic, and Tarim Basin chokepoint respectively). The robust frontiers between these terrain types reasserted themselves as soon as the centralizing pressure of Mongol unification weakened. The khanates' specific borders track the robust frontiers the Mongol synthesis had temporarily overridden.

**The four-confirmations geography reading** in Chapter 7 is structurally identical to Anthony's robust-frontier mechanism. Geography selects for adaptations; bundles of opposed customs form on each side; the frontier persists until or unless terrain features change. The dissertation has been documenting this empirically. Anthony has named the mechanism in a publishable definitional sentence.
