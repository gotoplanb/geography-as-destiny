# Falsifiability, Counterfactuals, and the Limits of Probabilistic History

**Source:** Alpha discussion, 2026-04-12
**Type:** connection
**Relevant to:** Chapter 1 (The Distribution — methodology section), Prologue

## Content

The book borrows scientific framing — probability distributions, falsifiability, counterfactual testing — but cannot deliver clean experimental data. There is no sample size. There are no controlled experiments. History gives you one run of the tape.

This needs to be addressed head-on in the methodology, not dodged. The argument:

**What we borrow from science:**
- **Falsifiability.** For the geographic-determinism thesis to be a theory (not a belief), there must be situations where it doesn't hold. If geography explained everything, it wouldn't be a theory — it would be a tautology. The book must identify cases where geography *doesn't* predict the outcome, and explain why (great man outliers, friction collapses, cultural drift). The framework is stronger for having boundaries.
- **Counterfactual testing.** For any great man claim: remove the person, ask if the structural outcome changes. For any geographic claim: change the terrain feature, ask if the outcome changes. This is the closest thing to an experimental method available in historical analysis.
- **Probabilistic language.** Distributions, not determinism. Likelihoods, not certainties. This is the honest vocabulary for claims you can't prove with a p-value.

**What we can't do:**
- We can't generate actual distribution curves with real data. The sample size is one (history happened once). We can gesture at what the distribution looks like, and we can use parallel cases (Russia/America, Nile/Yellow River) as quasi-replication, but we can't plot it.
- We can't run controlled experiments. We can't rerun the 19th century without the railroad and see what happens. Counterfactuals are thought experiments, not experiments.

**What we claim instead:**
- The intellectual rigor of applying these frameworks — falsifiability, counterfactual removal, probabilistic language — even without clean data, produces better history than the alternative (narrative assertion without any methodological controls). Most macro history doesn't even ask "could I be wrong?" We do.
- The parallel cases (same geography + same technology = same outcome, independently) function as natural experiments. Not clean ones. But the convergence is more than anecdote and less than proof. It's evidence at the level history can provide.

**The belief vs. theory distinction (Dave's framing):**
- In common usage, "theory" means "my opinion with some logic attached." In scientific usage, a theory is falsifiable — there are conditions under which it fails. The book uses "theory" in the scientific sense. The geographic framework makes predictions that can be checked: similar terrains should produce similar outcomes. When they don't, the framework needs to explain why, not ignore the exception.

## Why this matters

This is the intellectual backbone of Chapter 1. The reader who takes the book seriously will ask: "How is this different from just telling a story that confirms your thesis?" The answer is: falsifiability and counterfactual discipline. We name the conditions under which we'd be wrong. That's what separates this from Jared Diamond at his weakest (where geographic determinism can feel like just-so stories).

- Probabilistic reasoning in historical analysis (possibly Philip Tetlock, *Expert Political Judgment*, 2005 — Tetlock's work on forecasting and calibration applies the probabilistic frame to political/historical prediction)
