# The Silk Road as Relay Trade Network, Not Road

**Source:** Hansen, *The Silk Road: A New History* (opening chapters), via Beta seminar 2026-04-15
**Type:** fact
**Relevant to:** Chapter 4 (Silk Road as Circulatory System)

## Content

The Silk Road was never a road. The name was coined by Ferdinand von Richthofen, a German geographer in the 1870s who was surveying potential railroad routes for German imperial interests. "Seidenstraße" (Silk Road) framing served railroad planning logic — a road implies infrastructure, directionality, intentionality. The name is a sender-side imposition on a receiver-side reality that was far more organic.

**The relay model:** Goods moved through a series of overlapping local networks, not end-to-end. Nobody traveled the full length. A merchant from the east might reach Samarkand but not continue to Iran. Goods pooled at nodes (Samarkand, Bukhara, Kashgar, Dunhuang), got exchanged, repackaged, and flowed onward. No coinage — barter connecting one set of interconnections to another.

**The geographic implication:** This strengthens the chokepoint thesis. If goods moved by relay, the nodes are where geographic logic concentrates. Every parcel had to pass through them. The node extracts value at every hop. The Silk Road isn't a road — it's a series of lakes connected by streams. Each lake is a trading city where goods pool. Nobody swims the whole length. But the water gets there.

## Why this matters

The relay model makes the chapter's argument sharper, not weaker. Chapter 4 (Silk Road as Circulatory System) should present the corridor not as a trade route but as a self-organizing network of nodes connected by lines of least geographic resistance. The nodes are the story. The connections between them are determined by terrain.
