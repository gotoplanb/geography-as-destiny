# Horse as Friction Collapse — Quantified

**Source:** Hämäläinen, *The Comanche Empire*, Ch. 2 (audiobook reverse timestamp: 15h24m remaining — use to locate page in print edition)
**Type:** fact
**Relevant to:** Chapter 6 (Parallel Conquests — empirical anchor for horse-as-friction-collapse)

## Content

Hämäläinen provides specific load capacity figures for horse adoption on the Plains circa 1710:

- **Dog + travois:** ~75 lbs maximum (pre-horse baseline)
- **Horse carrying:** 200 lbs of cargo
- **Horse + travois:** 300 lbs dragged

**4x increase in cargo capacity, almost overnight.** Not gradual improvement. Step-change discontinuity in the energy cost of moving material across the Plains.

The travois is the key comparison. Pre-horse Plains cultures already had transportation technology (dogs + travois). The horse didn't introduce a new concept. It plugged into existing infrastructure and multiplied capacity by 4x. That's Rogers: compatible with existing practice, dramatically superior performance, low integration cost, immediately visible benefit. Rapid adoption predicted and observed.

**The railroad comparison for Chapter 6:**
- Dog + travois: ~75 lbs
- Horse + travois: ~300 lbs (4x over dog)
- Single railroad freight car: ~100 tons / 200,000 lbs (670x over horse)

Two friction collapses, both quantifiable. The horse enabled Comanche empire (4x cargo capacity = mobile surplus economy viable). The railroad ended it (670x cargo capacity = horse economically irrelevant). The contrast in magnitude explains the sequence: horse creates the empire, railroad destroys it.

## Why this matters

1. **Quantified friction collapse is stronger than described friction collapse.** Chapter 1 and Chapter 6 have been arguing friction collapse through narrative and logic. This card provides actual numbers — a 4x cargo capacity increase at horse adoption, a 670x increase at railroad adoption. The argument moves from "the technology changed the terrain's function" to "here is the measured magnitude of the change."

2. **The dog-travois baseline matters.** The Comanche weren't starting from zero mobility. They had a functioning transportation system. The horse was a 4x multiplier on an existing system — which is why adoption was so fast (Rogers: low integration cost, compatible with existing practice). This distinguishes horse adoption on the Plains from horse domestication on the Eurasian steppe (ref 100) where the starting point was different.

3. **The sequential magnitude explains the outcome.** Horse: 4x over dog = empire viable. Railroad: 670x over horse = empire obsolete. The second number is so much larger than the first that no institutional adaptation could compensate. The Comanche couldn't adapt to the railroad the way they adapted to the horse because the magnitude was categorically different.
