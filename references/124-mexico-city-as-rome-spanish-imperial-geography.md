# Mexico City as Rome — Spanish Imperial Geography and the North-South Asymmetry

**Source:** Beta session synthesis, 2026-05-03
**Type:** connection
**Relevant to:** Chapter 6 (Parallel Conquests — Spanish empire as geographic determinism case), ref 121 (Comanche diplomatic breakdown), ref 103 (mountain orientation)

## Content

**The Rome parallel:** Mexico City — built on Tenochtitlan — is positioned almost identically to Rome on the Italian peninsula in one structural sense: a defensible central location from which power can be projected in multiple directions without any single frontier being existentially threatening, because the others provide strategic depth. High-altitude valley, lake-based defensibility, temperate climate, mountain ranges on multiple sides. The Aztecs chose it for exactly these reasons. Spain inherited both the location and the administrative infrastructure the Aztecs had built around it.

The difference from Rome: Rome had the Mediterranean as a connective highway in all directions, making power projection cheap. Spain had ocean on two sides and hostile terrain on the others. Roman logistics were maritime-subsidized. Spanish logistics were overland-expensive. This cost differential partially explains why Spain burned out faster than Rome despite comparable territorial ambitions.

**The north-south supply line asymmetry:** From Mexico City, the empire's geographic logic bifurcates sharply:

*North (New Mexico, Texas):* Difficult but continuous. Overland through increasingly arid but manageable terrain, along established indigenous trade routes. Long distances from Mexico City to Santa Fe, but navigable with horses and pack animals through known corridors. The Comanche corridor problem (ref 121) is downstream of this — Spain could project power north, but the supply line was long enough that a disrupted frontier like New Mexico became very hard to defend.

*South (South America):* A fundamentally different problem. Competing with Portugal in Brazil requires either maritime capacity down the Atlantic coast or overland routes through the Amazon basin and the Andes. The Amazon is the anti-Nile: too much water, wrong kind, no manageable floodplain — it drains a continent but enables nothing. The Andes are oriented north-south, which helps movement along the spine but creates enormous east-west friction for any transverse route.

**Portugal got Brazil by default:** The Treaty of Tordesillas divided the Americas on paper. Geography divided them in practice. Spain's center of gravity was pulling northwest — toward the defensible Mexican highlands and the relatively continuous overland terrain leading north. Brazil fell outside Spain's natural power projection arc. Portugal, a maritime power with Atlantic-facing supply lines and no Andean barrier between itself and the Brazilian coast, was structurally better positioned for the terrain it got. The paper treaty ratified what geography had already decided.

**The strategic depth mechanism:** Spain from Mexico City could absorb frontier pressure from any single direction because pressure from one side didn't threaten the center while the other frontiers were quiet. This is the same logic as Rome absorbing Germanic pressure in the north while the Mediterranean south remained stable. The existential threat only materializes when multiple frontiers are stressed simultaneously — which is exactly what eventually happened to Spain as British and French colonial competition escalated, Comanche pressure grew on the northern frontier, and silver revenues from the mines declined.

## Why this matters

1. **Chapter 6 geographic frame.** The Spanish parallel conquest chapter needs a geographic foundation for why Spain's empire looked the way it did. Mexico City as a Rome-equivalent defensible center explains the empire's shape — including why New Mexico was a frontier Spain could barely hold rather than a core it could easily defend.

2. **The Amazon as anti-Nile formulation.** Chapter 6 or the framework sections can use this directly: the Nile enabled Egypt, the Yellow River enabled China, the Amazon enables nothing. Too much water, wrong kind, no floodplain agriculture, no corridor function. The Amazon's geographic failure is as structurally important as the great rivers' geographic success.

3. **Treaty of Tordesillas as geographic determinism confirmation.** The paper division roughly tracked what geography made possible. This is a clean falsification test — if politics, not geography, determined colonial boundaries, the division should have looked arbitrary relative to terrain. It didn't. Portugal got the terrain its maritime logistics could reach; Spain got the terrain its overland logistics could reach.
