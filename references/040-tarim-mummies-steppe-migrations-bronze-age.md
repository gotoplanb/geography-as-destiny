# Tarim Mummies, Steppe Migrations, and the Bronze Age Collapse

**Source:** Hansen, *The Silk Road* (early chapters on Tarim Basin archaeology), via Beta seminar 2026-04-15
**Type:** fact
**Relevant to:** Chapter 4 (Silk Road — deep time context), general framework (pre-literate migration as thermodynamic flow)

## Content

**The Tarim mummies:** Extraordinarily well-preserved bodies from the Tarim Basin, ~2000–500 BCE, with western Eurasian physical characteristics. Hansen discusses these as evidence of the corridor's deep history of human movement.

**EVIDENCE-STRENGTH WARNING:** Recent ancient DNA analysis (~2021) showed these individuals were genetically isolated — descendants of an Ancient North Eurasian population, not recent migrants from the Caspian steppe. This *contradicts* the intuitive "they migrated from the west" reading of their physical appearance. The science has moved beyond the visual evidence. Be careful not to build on the migration narrative when the genetics say isolation.

**The broader Yamnaya expansion:** The Pontic-Caspian steppe Yamnaya culture expanded ~3000–2500 BCE, spreading Indo-European languages from Europe to South Asia. This IS well-supported by genetics and linguistics. But it's a separate event from the Tarim mummies — don't conflate them.

**The Sea Peoples connection:** Dave's instinct that the Bronze Age Collapse (~1200 BCE) Sea Peoples may connect to Caspian steppe populations is speculative. Beta flagged it as "not crazy but contested." The Sea Peoples were almost certainly a coalition of displaced groups, not a single steppe population. This is an area where the framework (climate pressure → population movement → collapse of trade networks) makes predictions, but the specific evidence is thin.

**What's solid for the book:** Pre-literate population movements followed the same thermodynamic logic as later historical movements. Climate shifts on the steppe increased pressure on nomadic populations. The pressure found the path of least resistance. People flowed toward resource gradients. "The river was running before anyone was writing about it." That claim is well-supported by archaeology and genetics broadly, even if specific connections (Tarim-to-Sea-Peoples) are speculative.

## Why this matters

1. **Chapter 4** can use the Tarim Basin's deep archaeological record to establish that the Silk Road corridor was active long before anyone called it a Silk Road — and long before any empire tried to control it. The geography was already concentrating human activity at the same nodes.

2. **Methodological caution.** This is exactly the kind of material where the framework makes you see patterns that may not be there. Steppe migrations → Bronze Age Collapse → Sea Peoples is a beautiful chain that follows the thermodynamic logic perfectly. That's why it needs extra scrutiny. The framework should predict patterns that the evidence confirms, not generate patterns the evidence hasn't confirmed yet.
