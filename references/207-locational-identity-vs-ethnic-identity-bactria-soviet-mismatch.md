## Calibration Note

The empirical claims in this card are largely Category 1-2 — aligned with current scholarship on Soviet nationality policy and Central Asian regional identity, with framework-internal vocabulary ("locational identity," "gravity well function") layered onto well-documented historical patterns. Three calibrations baked into the framing:

1. **"Continuous gravity well function for 4000 years" reads as iterative-with-persistent-structural-position, not literal demographic continuity.** What's continuous is the *structural position* the terrain organizes — multi-system commercial node at the Zeravshan/Bactria corridor. The populations, languages, and religious substrates change across phases (BMAC ~2200-1700 BCE → Achaemenid Bactria 6th c BCE → Hellenistic Bactria → Kushan → Sogdian commerce → Samanid → Bukharan Emirate → Soviet → contemporary). The gravity-well function persists; the specific demographic and cultural content adapts.

2. **The locational-identity claim operates at substrate level, not individual self-identification level.** The framework can defend: terrain-organized commercial-node positions produce regional identity substrates that persist across political overlays because the terrain regenerates the conditions making the position salient. The framework cannot defend without anthropological specifics: that any individual Bactrian in 2000 BCE recognized continuous identity with a Bactrian in 1800 CE. The continuity is structural-position-recurrence, not individual self-identification across millennia.

3. **Soviet nationality policy scholarship is consensus.** Adeeb Khalid (*Making Uzbekistan*, 2015), Francine Hirsch (*Empire of Nations*, 2005), and Terry Martin (*The Affirmative Action Empire*, 2001) all document the 1924 National Delimitation as imposing ethnic categories that didn't match lived identity in significant ways for significant populations. The "Bactrians felt Bactrian not Uzbek/Tajik" framing comes from Tasar's lecture material; the underlying scholarship supports the broader claim that Soviet categories were poorly matched to existing regional substrates.

## The Framework Move

**Locational identity and ethnic/lineage identity are distinct organizing principles for population self-categorization. Terrain that organizes a sustained multi-system commercial gravity well produces locational identity. Modern administrative systems imposing lineage-identity categories on locational-identity populations produce systemic mismatch and persistent conflict.**

Locational identity:
- Defined by position in a commercial-cultural network rather than by inherited lineage
- Produced by terrain organizing the same structural conditions across multiple political iterations
- Adapts specific content (language, religion, political affiliation) across phases while preserving network-position substrate
- Multilingual, multi-religious, and multi-political-affiliation as adaptive features rather than transitional states
- Persists as long as the terrain continues regenerating the gravity-well conditions

Ethnic/lineage identity (the principle Soviet nationality policy operationalized):
- Defined by claimed descent through a continuous demographic lineage
- Requires identifiable boundary criteria (language, religion, ancestry) that distinguish in-group from out-group
- Treats multilingualism, multi-religious affiliation, or shifting political identity as anomalies or transitional states
- Stable through the politics of nation-state administration, unstable when the boundary criteria fail to map cleanly to populations

The two principles use incompatible vocabularies. When an administrative system organized around ethnic/lineage identity attempts to register a population organized around locational identity, the system generates categorical errors and the population experiences sustained dissonance with the imposed labels.

Each period imposes a different political vocabulary on the population. The Bactrian *gravity-well function* persists. The Bactrian *locational identity substrate* persists as the regional-affiliation layer underneath whatever political overlay is current.

A Bactrian in 2000 BCE describing identity would have used vocabulary unrecognizable to a Bactrian in 1800 CE. But the structural position — *I am from the place where the routes cross, where multiple commercial systems converge, where multilingualism and multi-religious affiliation are operating conditions of daily life* — recurs across every political iteration because the terrain that organizes that position doesn't change.

## The Soviet Categorical Mismatch

Soviet nationality policy emerged in the 1920s under Stalin's nationalities commissar role and was operationalized through the 1924 National Delimitation (card 028). The policy imposed ethnic-categorical identity (Uzbek, Tajik, Turkmen, Kazakh, Kyrgyz) on populations who had organized their identity around different principles for millennia. Adeeb Khalid documents how the categories were partly constructed during the delimitation process itself — Soviet ethnographers and Central Asian intellectuals together producing the "ethnic" boundaries that the policy would then administer.

The Bactrian-region populations experienced this categorization as foreign because the imposed framework used **lineage vocabulary on a population whose identity had been locational**. The categories asked: "What is your ancestry? What is your ethnic group? What is your nation?" The locational identity substrate answered different questions: "Where are you from? What network do you operate in? What languages and traditions does your position require?"

The category mismatch produced and continues to produce the conflicts Tasar's course documents — Tajik-Uzbek border tensions, Ferghana Valley ethnic violence, the persistent low-level conflict over which contemporary republic any specific Bactrian-region community "belongs to." The conflicts are not primarily about ancient ethnic grievances. They are about administrative systems organized around the wrong identity principle generating sustained dissonance with populations whose actual identity organization predates the administrative vocabulary by millennia.

The framework's reading: **the Soviet nationality policy attempted to replace a four-millennium-iterative locational identity substrate with a five-decade-old ethnic administrative category.** The locational substrate didn't yield. It reasserted itself precisely because the imposed categories couldn't fully replace what the terrain kept regenerating.
