# Global Coverage as Anti-Cherry-Picking Discipline

**Source:** Alpha discussion, 2026-04-13
**Type:** connection
**Relevant to:** General — table of contents evolution, all chapters

## Content

The thesis claims geography sets the distribution everywhere. If the book only covers Eurasia and North America, it's vulnerable to the cherry-picking objection — "you picked the cases that confirm your framework."

Every region of the world is either additional confirmation or an honest counter-case. Both strengthen the book.

**Coverage strategy:** Not every region needs its own chapter. Cases can enter as evidence within existing chapters:
- **South America:** Amazon as anti-Nile (Ch. 2), Inca as rotated-river analog (Ch. 2), Aztec as manufactured delta (Ch. 2)
- **Sub-Saharan Africa:** could enter through the chokepoint chapter, the diffusion chapter (Bantu expansion as Rogers case?), or the river spine (Niger, Congo)
- **Southeast Asia:** Scott's Zomia (*Art of Not Being Governed*) is already in the lit review — non-adoption as rational strategy
- **Oceania:** Polynesian navigation as geographic epistemology (ref 001), island civilizations as extreme geographic constraint
- **East Asia:** Qing expansion into Central Asia as parallel to Russian/American expansion (ref 002), Yellow River as river-spine case

The table of contents will evolve as semesters fill in the globe. By semester 5, every major region should appear somewhere in the evidence base. If it doesn't, that's a signal to investigate — either the framework doesn't apply there (which needs to be addressed) or we haven't looked (which is cherry-picking).

## Why this matters

This is a structural discipline, not a content decision. The book's credibility depends on the reader believing the cases were chosen for evidentiary strength, not for confirmatory convenience. Global coverage is the antidote.
