## Calibration Update (added 2026-05-13) — Geography and Descent

Two corrections without disturbing the triangulation methodology argument:

**1. Afanasievo geography.** The original card placed Afanasievo "in the Altai-Sayan region." More precisely, Afanasievo (~3300-2500 BCE) is centered in the **Minusinsk Basin (upper Yenisei drainage)**, between the Sayan and Altai mountain ranges. "Altai-Sayan region" is loose but not catastrophic; the corrected location is just east via the steppe spine. The eastward-leapfrog mechanism is unaffected.

**2. Afanasievo→Tocharian descent.** The original card asserted that "The Afanasievo population eventually moved south into the Tarim Basin and became the Tocharians" as established fact. This is **one hypothesis** (Mallory/Anthony lineage) but is contested by recent ancient DNA evidence (Zhang et al. 2021 *Nature*; Damgaard et al. 2018). Zhang et al. found that the earliest Tarim Basin populations (Xiaohe horizon, ~2100-1700 BCE) were a genetic isolate descended primarily from Ancient North Eurasian ancestry, with **no detectable Western Steppe (Yamnaya/Afanasievo) ancestry**. The Tocharian-language carriers may have arrived later through a different mechanism than direct Afanasievo descent.

This complicates but does not break the triangulation methodology. The eastward expansion of Yamnaya-affiliated populations is documented (Afanasievo in the Minusinsk Basin is real). The continuity to the Tocharian-speaking populations of the first-millennium-CE Tarim oasis cities is more complex than a single demographic pulse and may involve multiple later population movements layering Indo-European-speaking groups onto an earlier genetic isolate.

**Framework impact: triangulation methodology preserved; specific descent claim weakened.** The "PIE bridges Bronze Age to Silk Road" connection works at the language/cultural-substrate level but cannot rest on a clean Yamnaya→Afanasievo→Tarim demographic pulse. A more honest framing: Yamnaya expansion produces an eastward outpost (Afanasievo, Minusinsk Basin); the Tocharian-speaking populations of the Tarim Basin oasis cities emerge through more complex mechanisms including possibly later Indo-Iranian-speaking groups arriving and being absorbed into an earlier genetic substrate.

The card content below should be read in light of this correction.

## The Triangulation Methodology

Anthony's chapter 6 is identifying the PIE homeland by working backwards from multiple simultaneous expansion waves moving in different directions. The methodology is structurally identical to signal triangulation from multiple receivers. Each expansion wave is a receiver. The homeland is the transmitter. Find where all the waves originated and you have found the source.

This is a generalizable framework methodology worth naming explicitly. Wherever the framework needs to identify the originating zone of a pressure wave or innovation, it can use the same triangulation move: identify multiple downstream cases at distinct geographic endpoints, trace each backward through the timing and signature of its arrival, and locate the convergence point where the traces meet.

Three properties make the triangulation methodology rigorous:

1. **Multiple independent expansion vectors.** A single expansion direction doesn't constrain the homeland tightly — the source could be anywhere along the vector's projection backwards. Multiple vectors moving in different directions intersect at the homeland.

2. **Timing convergence.** The expansion waves should be approximately contemporaneous if they originate from the same source population at the same demographic-pressure event. Significant timing divergence suggests separate origin events rather than a single triangulable source.

3. **Signature consistency.** The genetic, archaeological, and linguistic signatures arriving at each downstream endpoint should share enough common features to identify them as variants of the same originating population. Wildly divergent signatures suggest independent sources, not a single triangulable origin.

Anthony's case meets all three properties. Multiple expansion vectors (west into Europe, east toward the Altai, south toward the Caucasus). Contemporaneous arrivals (~3000-2500 BCE across the different endpoints). Consistent signatures (Yamnaya genetic affinity, Indo-European linguistic ancestry, similar burial practices, similar material culture markers).

## The Westward Push

Yamnaya ancestry appears in European populations starting around 3000-2500 BCE, overlaying the earlier Anatolian farmer substrate (card 169's middle wave). The cultural manifestations:

- **Corded Ware culture** (~2900-2350 BCE) in northern and central Europe. Strong Yamnaya genetic signature. Single burial practices similar to Yamnaya kurgan tradition. Material culture markers traceable to steppe origins.
- **Bell Beaker culture** (~2800-1800 BCE) extending further west into Iberia, the British Isles, and parts of the Mediterranean. Yamnaya-derived genetic component, mixed with regional substrates.

The westward expansion is the most thoroughly documented in current genetic and archaeological literature. It is also the one that produced the populations the Western civilization curriculum descends from — which means it has received the most scholarly attention, which means it has the most accumulated evidence.

## The Eastward Push and the Tocharian Bridge

The eastward expansion is the one that connects Anthony's Bronze Age material directly to the dissertation's existing Silk Road / Tarim Basin material — and the bridge is the Tocharians.

**Afanasievo culture (~3300-2500 BCE)** in the Altai-Sayan region is the earliest known steppe culture east of the Urals. Its material culture, burial practices, and genetic signature show direct Yamnaya affinity, suggesting it arrived as an eastward expansion of the same Volga-Don population that pushed west into Europe at roughly the same time.

The Afanasievo population eventually moved south into the Tarim Basin and became the **Tocharians** — speakers of Tocharian A and Tocharian B, the easternmost Indo-European languages, attested in documents recovered from the Tarim Basin oasis cities (Kucha, Turfan, Yanqi, Dunhuang region) dating to the late first millennium CE.

**This is the bridge that connects the Anthony reading arc to the dissertation's existing Silk Road material.** The Tarim Basin oasis cities the framework has been reading as Silk Road chokepoint civilizations were populated, in part, by the descendants of an eastward Yamnaya expansion that originated in the Volga-Don steppe around 3000 BCE. The PIE substrate the framework has been tracing through Anthony's chapter 6 reaches its eastern terminus in the same corridor where Chapter 4's Silk Road material was developed.
