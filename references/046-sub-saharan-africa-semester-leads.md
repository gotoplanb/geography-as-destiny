# Sub-Saharan Africa — Regional Leads for Future Semester

**Source:** Beta seminar discussion, 2026-04-16
**Type:** connection
**Relevant to:** Global coverage (ref 019), future semester planning

## Content

Sub-Saharan Africa is almost entirely absent from the Western historical imagination and from this project so far. Beta identified four geographic zones that map onto the framework:

**1. The Congo Basin = the Amazon problem.** Too much water, wrong soil chemistry, impenetrable canopy. The framework predicts no river-spine civilization here, and it doesn't produce one — same logic as ref 016.

**2. The East African Rift Valley + Great Lakes (Tanganyika, Victoria, Malawi).** A chain of great lakes creating the linear water feature the framework identifies as civilization-generating. The Great Lakes region supported sophisticated pre-colonial kingdoms — Buganda, Rwanda. This is the river-spine model applied to a lake chain, which is interesting given the lake-vs-river geometry argument (ref 021) — a *chain* of lakes may function more like a river than a single lake does.

**3. The Swahili coast.** Where the Indian Ocean trade network touched East Africa. Cosmopolitan trading cities that were essentially the eastern African equivalent of Silk Road oasis cities. Same relay trade logic, same chokepoint dynamic. Almost completely unknown to Western readers. Potential Chapter 4 or Chapter 5 material — the Indian Ocean as a parallel corridor to the Silk Road.

**4. The West African Sahel.** The band just south of the Sahara. The medieval empires of Mali, Ghana, Songhai controlled the transition zone between desert and savanna, extracting value from trans-Saharan trade routes the way Transoxiana extracted value from the Silk Road. Gold and salt north, manufactured goods south. Same chokepoint logic, different terrain.

## Why this matters

1. **Global coverage discipline.** The book can't only cover Eurasia and North America. Africa has at least four distinct geographic zones that the framework should address. A dedicated Africa semester is probably necessary.

2. **The Sahel-as-steppe parallel** is striking — same transition-zone-as-chokepoint logic, same empire-building pattern, different geography. Mali/Ghana/Songhai controlling the Sahel is structurally identical to Transoxiana controlling the Silk Road corridor.

3. **The Swahili coast** opens the Indian Ocean trade network as a parallel to the Silk Road — a maritime corridor with relay trade, chokepoint cities, and geographic logic driving the pattern. This could be a chapter in its own right or evidence within the existing Silk Road chapter.

## Evidence-strength note

All of this is Beta synthesis, not sourced. Needs actual African history scholarship before building on it. Potential sources to investigate:
- John Iliffe, *Africans: The History of a Continent* (2007) — general survey
- Chapurukha Kusimba, *The Rise and Fall of Swahili States* (1999) — Swahili coast
- Nehemia Levtzion, *Ancient Ghana and Mali* (1973) — Sahel empires
- The Great Courses has an Africa history survey that could serve as a seminar source
