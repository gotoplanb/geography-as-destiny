# The Maritime Silk Road — Ocean as Ultimate Low-Friction Corridor

**Source:** Hansen, *The Silk Road* (Sri Lankan pearls at Niya), via Beta seminar 2026-04-19
**Type:** connection
**Relevant to:** Chapter 4 (Silk Road — the missing half), ref 035 (relay trade), ref 036 (Richthofen bias)

## Content

Sri Lankan pearls at Niya almost certainly didn't cross the Karakoram. They traveled the maritime route:
- Sri Lanka → Bay of Bengal → Southeast Asian coast → South China Sea → Chinese ports
- Then potentially redistributed westward overland

The maritime Silk Road ran simultaneously with the overland routes and in many periods carried more volume. The ocean is the ultimate low-friction corridor: no terrain to navigate, wind-powered (essentially free energy), capable of carrying enormous cargo weight. The thermodynamic logic strongly favors maritime over mountain whenever both options exist.

**The Myanmar/Irrawaddy corridor:** The Irrawaddy River in Burma is one of the ancient connectors between the Indian subcontinent and Southeast Asia. Lower friction than the Karakoram by a significant margin — no passes above 15,000 feet, tropical rather than arctic.

**The Richthofen bias, again (ref 036):** Why do we talk about the Silk Road as primarily overland when maritime routes were arguably more economically significant? Because Richthofen was planning a railroad. His sender-side encoding emphasized the overland corridor. The maritime routes got underemphasized in the popular imagination. The pearls knew the easier route even if the historians followed the harder one.

**Friction-as-content-filter extended:** The overland routes filtered for lightweight, high-value content (ref 052). The maritime routes had no such filter — you can ship pearls, spices, bulk ceramics, timber, anything. The ocean's low friction means the content selection is determined by demand, not by transport constraints. This is why maritime trade carried more volume: the channel had higher bandwidth.

## Why this matters

1. **Chapter 4 is incomplete without the maritime half.** The Silk Road chapter currently emphasizes the overland corridor because that's what Tasar covered. Hansen is providing the eastern half. But the maritime routes are the southern half — and by volume, possibly the more significant half. The chapter needs both.

2. **The ocean as channel type.** The framework has discussed rivers, steppe, mountains, deserts. The ocean is a distinct channel type: low friction, high bandwidth, but high latency (ref 013 — sail era). It carries everything but slowly. Different channel characteristics, different diffusion profile than overland routes.

3. **Southeast Asia enters the evidence base.** The maritime route connects Sri Lanka, the Bay of Bengal, Myanmar, Thailand (Kra Isthmus), Vietnam, and southern China. This is new geographic territory for the project — addresses the global coverage discipline (ref 019).

4. **Pearl distribution as friction map.** Where pearls show up tells you which route they took. Pearls at Niya but not along the Karakoram trail = maritime-to-overland redistribution. The commodity distribution maps the route friction without needing any written record.
