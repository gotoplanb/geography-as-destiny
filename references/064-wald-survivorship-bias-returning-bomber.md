# Wald's Returning Bomber — Survivorship Bias Applied to Historiography

**Source:** Beta seminar discussion, 2026-04-19
**Type:** connection
**Relevant to:** Chapter 1 (methodology — possibly the clearest illustration), Prologue, ref 048 (survivor bias as history), ref 063 (absence = friction)

## Content

Abraham Wald, Statistical Research Group, World War II. The military showed him bullet hole patterns on returning bombers and proposed reinforcing the damaged areas. Wald said: reinforce where there are *no* holes. The planes that took hits there didn't return. The sample is contaminated by the selection mechanism of survival itself.

**Applied to historiography:**

The archaeological record is the returning bomber. What survives at Niya, in the Dunhuang caves, in the Sogdian merchant letters — those are the bullet holes on the planes that made it back. The absence of Kharoshthi documents along the mountain route isn't a gap. Those are the engine hits. The silence is where the fatal shots landed.

Every historical source is a returning bomber. The historian who reads only the sources that survived is the simpleton designer adding armor to the wings.

- This card: Wald's framework unifies all three — the methodology is the study of what didn't return

**The receiver-side methodology restated:** The most important information is in what didn't survive, what wasn't transmitted, what got filtered out by the friction of the channel. Geography is how you reconstruct the missing planes — the terrain tells you which routes were survivable and which weren't. The record we have is the sample that made it back. The geography tells you about the sample that didn't.
