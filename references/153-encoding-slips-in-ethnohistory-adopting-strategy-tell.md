# Encoding Slips in Ethnohistory — The "Adopting a Strategy" Tell

**Source:** Beta session synthesis (Dave Stanton observation), 2026-05-09
**Type:** methodology / source-reading framework
**Relevant to:** Chapter 1 (framework / receiver-side reading), Chapter 6 (Parallel Conquests), ref 053 (tributary system as sender-receiver encoding), ref 064 (Wald survivorship-bias / returning bomber), ref 095 (phase-dependent encoding / Genghis), ref 133 (decentralized nomadic authority), ref 148 (hypocrisy as encoding mismatch — methodology), ref 152 (Medicine Lodge treaty encoding gap)

## The Observation

Hämäläinen is a careful and sympathetic historian whose *Comanche Empire* is the dissertation's primary scholarly source for the Plains material. Her revisionist project — taking the Comanche seriously as an imperial polity rather than as raiding obstacles to American expansion — is methodologically rigorous and analytically generative.

And yet the American legal and political framework is so deeply embedded in the documentary record she works from that it occasionally shapes her framing in ways that reproduce the encoding she is otherwise trying to correct. Two specific examples in the post-Medicine Lodge material illustrate the pattern.

## Slip 1 — "Adopting a Strategy" of Dual Reservation Use

After Medicine Lodge, Hämäläinen describes the Comanche as *adopting* a dual-use pattern in which some bands centralized around the reservation as a resource store while others continued operating on the grasslands. The verb is the encoding slip. *Adopting* implies invention or creative adaptation to a novel circumstance — the reservation as primary reality, Comanche behavior as adjustment to it.

The framework's reading: the Comanche were *continuing* a pattern they had always run. Fixed nodes used as resource access points while the broader confederation maintained mobility across territory had been the Comanche economic structure for generations. The Big Timbers trading center on the Arkansas, the seasonal trade fairs in New Mexico, the Spanish mission settlements they traded with — all functioned structurally identically to the reservation. Each was a fixed node in a mobile network. The reservation was just the newest such node, signed for under American legal forms but operationally continuous with everything that came before.

The slip happens because the documentary record describes the reservation as a permanent residential assignment, and from that vantage Comanche presence on it looks like compliance and Comanche absence from it looks like deviation. The verb *adopt* sneaks in because the writer is momentarily reading the reservation as the document said it was rather than as the Comanche were actually using it.

## Slip 2 — "Split Into Two"

Hämäläinen also describes the post-Medicine Lodge confederation as *splitting* — three bands centralized around the reservation, three others spent most of their time on the grasslands. The framing implies a unified entity dividing under pressure.

The Comanche were never a unified entity in the American political sense. They were a confederation of bands with distributed decision-making, in which different bands had always made different choices about relationships with different neighbors. Some bands had always maintained closer ties with Spanish settlements. Others operated more independently. Different responses to different reservations is not a *split*. It is the confederation's normal distributed decision-making becoming visible to observers who had been incorrectly assuming unified sovereignty.

The slip happens because the American framework expected one Comanche counterparty for the treaty, and behavior that did not conform to single-counterparty expectations registered as fragmentation. The framing recodes confederation-normal behavior as confederation-collapse behavior. Card 133 (decentralized nomadic authority as cultural substrate solution) is exactly this distinction stated as framework principle. The post-Medicine Lodge "split" was the American expectation discovering the confederation structure that had always been there.

## The Mechanism

Both slips share the same structure. The historian is working primarily from American administrative records — treaty documents, BIA correspondence, Army reports, settler accounts. These records encode Comanche behavior through American institutional frameworks. The behavior described is real. The framing it is described through is American.

Even when a historian is consciously trying to correct the framing, the records' encoding exerts continuous gravitational pull on the prose. Verbs like *adopt*, *split*, *break*, *comply* are calibrated to the American institutional reading and slip into the writing whenever the historian is not actively resisting them.

This is the Wald returning-bomber problem in ethnohistory. The records that survive are mostly written by the dominant party's institutional apparatus. The records that don't survive — Comanche internal deliberations, oral traditions about the same events, the ongoing logic that made the reservation just-another-fixed-node from inside the confederation — are the engine hits. Even the best ethnohistorian is reading mostly returning bombers.

## What to Watch For

This card is methodological. Its job is to give the dissertation reader (and future researchers using the framework) a small set of encoding-slip tells to watch for in any historical source describing colonized or otherwise-dominated populations. Each tell signals that the source's framing has reverted to the dominant-party encoding even when the source is otherwise sympathetic:

- **Innovation verbs (*adopt, devise, invent*)** applied to behavior that was structurally continuous with the population's pre-contact economic or social patterns. If the behavior in question matches a pattern visible before the dominant party arrived, *continue* or *resume* is the analytically accurate verb. *Adopt* is a slip.

- **Unity verbs (*split, fragment, divide, fracture*)** applied to populations whose pre-contact organization was always plural. If the population was never a single political unit in the dominant party's sense, *splits* are usually the visibility of normal distributed decision-making to observers who were assuming unity.

- **Compliance/breach vocabulary** (*broke the treaty, violated the agreement, failed to comply*) applied without acknowledging the encoding gap that made the agreement mean different things to different signatories. Card 152 develops this specifically for treaties.

- **Strategy vocabulary** (*strategic adaptation, calculated response*) applied to behavior the population was already running before the dominant party arrived. The strategic frame is the dominant party's frame; the population may have been operating under non-strategic logic that the strategic frame is back-formulating.

- **Surprise vocabulary** (*unexpectedly, surprisingly, against expectations*) where the surprise is the dominant party's expectation being violated rather than the population's behavior being novel. The population was not surprising. The dominant party was incorrect.

When any of these tells appear in a source, the historian's framing has temporarily reverted to the dominant-party encoding, regardless of the historian's overall sympathy or analytical care. The framework's job is to recognize the reversion and translate the description back into the population's own continuous logic.

## What This Does Not Mean

This is not a critique of Hämäläinen. *Comanche Empire* remains the foundational scholarly text on Plains imperial history and the reason the dissertation can take the Comanche seriously as an imperial polity in Chapter 6. Catching encoding slips in a sympathetic and rigorous source is not the same as discrediting the source. It is the documentation that even the best work in this field operates under the constant gravitational pull of the surviving records' encoding, and that the framework's job is to apply consistent corrective pressure rather than to expect any single text to operate fully outside the encoding.

The corrective pressure is the framework's contribution. The sympathetic source is the foundation on which the corrective pressure operates. Both are necessary. Neither alone suffices.

## What This Implies for the Framework

The dissertation's analytical apparatus — Shannon-Rogers-Diamond synthesis, sender-receiver encoding, Wald survivorship-bias correction — is not only useful for reading historical actors. It is useful for reading the historians who study them. Source-reading methodology is itself a framework application. Where the source's framing slips back into the dominant-party encoding, the framework can identify the slip and translate.

This makes the framework reflexively self-applying. It is not a perspective from outside historical scholarship that judges sources. It is a perspective that operates on sources the same way it operates on actors: by identifying the encoding apparatus and asking what is being lost when that encoding is treated as transparent.

**Even the best ethnohistorian is reading mostly returning bombers. The framework's job is to keep asking what the engine hits would have said.**
