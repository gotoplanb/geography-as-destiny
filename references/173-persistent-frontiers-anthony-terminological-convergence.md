Three independent scholars, three different evidentiary apparatuses, three convergent terminological choices for the framework's core argument. **The reading is unlikely to be artifact of any single observer's interpretive choices** when the same concept emerges from independent evidentiary paths.

## What "Persistent Frontiers" Names

The concept is precise. Frontiers that persist across millennia because the underlying geographic logic keeps regenerating them regardless of which specific cultures or empires are pressing against them. Not temporary boundaries that get resolved and disappear. Not lines that move steadily in one direction. Frontiers that the terrain reproduces structurally even when every specific actor on either side has been replaced.

Four examples worth tracking as the framework's clearest cases of persistent frontiers operating across documented history:

- **The Caucasus frontier** — Bronze Age (the friction-sieve that filtered Anatolian vocabulary into PIE, card 171) through classical antiquity (Roman vs Parthian) through Byzantine vs Arab through Russian vs Ottoman vs Persian through the Cold War's Iron Curtain through the current Russia-Georgia tensions and the Armenian-Azerbaijani conflict. Same mountain range. Different actors. Same persistent friction zone across approximately five thousand years.

- **The steppe-farming frontier** — Yamnaya pressure waves into Anatolian farming communities (card 169's three-wave model) through Scythian-Greek interactions through Han-Xiongnu through Byzantine-Turkic through Russian conquest of Central Asia (cards 009, 044) through Cold War Soviet-Chinese tensions through contemporary Russian-Ukrainian conflicts. The frontier moves slightly across centuries but persists at roughly the same latitudinal band where steppe pasture transitions to settled agriculture. Same transition zone. Different actors across five thousand years.

- **The Tarim Basin frontier** — Tocharian populations (card 172's Yamnaya-Afanasievo-Tocharian bridge) through the Tang Dynasty's pulsing frontier at the Jade Gate (cards 043, 109) through the Uyghur Khaganate through Tibetan expansion through Qing Dynasty conquest through the contemporary Xinjiang situation. Same chokepoint corridor. Different sovereignties. Same persistent frontier across two thousand years of documented history and at least three thousand years of inferable pre-documentary history.

- **The Zagros-Arabian desert corridor** — Sumerian frontier with the Iranian plateau through Akkadian through Persian-Babylonian through Sassanian-Roman through Arab Caliphate through Ottoman-Safavid through Sykes-Picot's failure to dissolve the underlying logic (card 117). Same terrain transition. Different ideological frames across five thousand years.

## What Distinguishes Persistent Frontiers From Temporary Borders

Card 130 began this distinction with the border-as-sedentary-technology argument — fixed lines are administrative technology; frontiers are pressure gradients. Card 173 sharpens it by specifying *what* persists:

- **The terrain feature** producing the frontier (mountain range, desert corridor, river spine, climate transition zone, chokepoint topology)
- **The asymmetry** between the populations the terrain feature separates (mobile vs settled, agricultural vs pastoralist, arid vs irrigated, etc.)
- **The friction profile** governing what crosses easily and what does not (high-value-density transmission across mountain passes, bulk commodity flow along river corridors, ideological coordination problems in pluralist contact zones)

The specific cultures, empires, languages, religions, and technologies change. The terrain feature, the asymmetry, and the friction profile persist. Persistent frontiers are not persistent because someone keeps deciding to maintain them. They are persistent because the geography keeps regenerating the conditions that produce them.

## What This Implies for the Framework

Three additions to the framework's apparatus:

1. **"Persistent frontiers" becomes a usable framework term.** The phrase is short enough to deploy in elevator-pitch contexts and precise enough to carry the framework's geographic-determinism argument. A reader who absorbs "persistent frontiers" has absorbed the dissertation's central position in two words. Future framework presentations — podcast appearances, conversational explanations, public-facing summaries — can deploy this term directly.

2. **The three-confirmation pattern is now established.** Future framework engagements with new scholarly work should track whether the new work converges on the framework's positions terminologically or analytically. Each new convergence is an independent confirmation. Each divergence is potential refinement. The framework can be candid about which is happening in any given case.

3. **The dissertation can claim independent-replication evidence for its core claim.** This is unusual for historical analysis. Most historical syntheses are evaluated against the evidence they cite rather than against independent convergent readings. The framework now has three independent convergent readings on its core position, from scholars working on different periods with different evidentiary apparatuses. That is the framework's strongest available defense against critics who would dismiss it as eccentric synthesis. It is not eccentric. It is a position multiple independent investigators have arrived at through their own work.

**Persistent frontiers. Geography as destiny. Same thing.**

Anthony's two-word chapter title is the dissertation's argument compressed to its minimum. Frontiers persist across millennia because the terrain that generates them does not change. The specific actors, technologies, and ideological justifications all change. The frontier persists. This is what geography determines. This is the framework's core claim. The framework is not eccentric.
