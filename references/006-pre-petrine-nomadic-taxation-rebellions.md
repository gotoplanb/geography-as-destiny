## Content

Before Peter the Great, Russia was already pushing south and east, blocking known nomadic highways and attempting to tax nomadic routes. Catherine sent Cossack forces to suppress rebellions caused by this pressure. The Kyrgyz (Russian generic name for steppe peoples) rebelled because:

- Russia was setting up toll stations to control nomadic routes
- The nomads didn't want sedentary life — they knew it existed, they just didn't want it
- Their culture was fundamentally different, shaped by the geography they'd lived in for millennia
- **Kenesary Qasymov** — grandson of Ablai Khan, assembled 20,000–50,000 men across the three Kazakh hordes, attacked Russian forts and caravans, was harsh toward other steppe peoples who wouldn't join. Killed in 1847 not by the Russians but by Kyrgyz who viewed him as a brigand. Same dynamic as Tecumseh.

## Why this matters

This is the pre-history of the conquest — the structural pressures already at play before Peter, before the railroad. Russia was already trying to make the steppe legible (taxation = legibility, per Scott). The nomads were already resisting. The pattern was set. The railroad just made the outcome inevitable rather than merely likely.

Also demonstrates that the sedentary/nomadic clash was not about ignorance — the nomads knew about sedentary life and chose otherwise. This connects to Scott's *The Art of Not Being Governed* — non-adoption as rational strategy, not backwardness. The steppe peoples were Rogers laggards by choice, not by ignorance.
