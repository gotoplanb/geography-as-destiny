## Calibration Note

Six calibrations baked in:

1. **The Huichang Suppression (842–845 CE) is well-documented.** Emperor Wuzong's persecution of Buddhism was the most severe anti-Buddhist campaign in Chinese history. Conventional figures (which may be partly canonical-narrative inflated): approximately 4,600 Buddhist monasteries destroyed; over 40,000 smaller shrines and temples destroyed; over 250,000 monks and nuns forcibly returned to lay life; substantial Buddhist properties confiscated and converted to state use. The persecution operated within Wuzong's broader Taoist-favoring political-cultural orientation and within the broader xenophobic-moral-revival apparatus that Han Yu's 819 CE memorial had articulated (per card 231). The Huichang persecution was substantially relaxed after Wuzong's death in 846 CE, but the Buddhist institutional infrastructure had been substantially weakened in ways the subsequent partial restoration did not fully repair.

2. **The Tang collapse 907 CE timing.** Zhu Wen's (later Emperor Taizu of Later Liang) deposition of the last Tang emperor (Ai Di) marked the formal end of the Tang dynasty in 907 CE. The Tang had been substantially fragmented for decades by warlord regional powers (jiedushi) following the late-9th-century crises (especially the Huang Chao Rebellion 875–884 CE). The Five Dynasties and Ten Kingdoms period (907–979 CE) followed; the Song reunification produced the next consolidated Chinese dynasty (per card 226's recursive structural parallel principle, the Song operates as another iteration of the dynastic-cycle apparatus). The Tang collapse marked the end of the political-administrative apparatus that had sustained the Silk Road's maximum function during the Tang cosmopolitan flowering period.

3. **The Dunhuang Cave 17 sealing date is contested but conventionally placed approximately 1000–1002 CE.** Cave 17 (also called the "Library Cave") of the Mogao Caves at Dunhuang was sealed during the late 10th or early 11th century CE. The cave contained tens of thousands of manuscripts in multiple languages (Chinese, Tibetan, Uighur, Sogdian, Khotanese, Sanskrit, and others), Buddhist artifacts, and various other materials. The specific sealing date is debated in modern scholarship; the popular range is roughly 1000–1002 CE based on the absence of post-1002 CE materials and the presence of materials up to that date.

4. **The reason for the sealing is contested in modern scholarship.** The popular narrative is anticipated Western Xia (Tangut) threat — the Western Xia kingdom (1038–1227 CE) was expanding in the region and conquered Dunhuang in 1036 CE; the sealing would have predated the conquest by approximately 35 years, suggesting anticipatory protection of religious materials from anticipated destruction. Alternative proposals include: routine archival storage that was simply forgotten when the apparatus that managed it ceased to function; deliberate hiding for specific cultural-religious reasons unrelated to Western Xia threat; consolidation of damaged or obsolete materials that local monastic apparatus chose to seal rather than discard; ritual-protective sealing for specific religious purposes. The framework's claim about the cave-as-time-capsule operates regardless of the specific sealing motivation; the broader analytical claim (substrate system that produced the materials had stopped functioning; local community preserved what they could of the apparatus the substrate had supported) operates robustly across the various specific motivation proposals.

5. **Hansen's reconstruction methodology.** Valerie Hansen's *The Silk Road: A New History* (2012) drew substantially on Dunhuang manuscript evidence (alongside other archaeological and documentary sources from Niya, Khotan, Turfan, Kucha, and other Silk Road sites) to reconstruct the Silk Road's actual operational dynamics. The Dunhuang materials provided documentary evidence for commercial transactions, religious practice, administrative operations, and cultural-linguistic diversity in ways that other source bases could not match. The framework's claim about Hansen's Dunhuang-based reconstruction operates on this well-documented scholarly apparatus.

6. **"The cave was sealed because the world that filled it had ended" is the framework's analytical articulation.** The framing operates at the broader substrate-claim level (the substrate system that produced the materials had stopped functioning) rather than at the specific-motivation level (which is debated per calibration 4). The framing should be flagged as framework analytical reading; the underlying claim about substrate-system transformation is well-grounded regardless of the specific sealing motivation.
