# Mountain Orientation: Tian Shan (East-West) vs. Rockies (North-South)

**Source:** Dave's prediction while reading Hämäläinen, 2026-04-23
**Type:** connection
**Relevant to:** Chapter 6 (Parallel Conquests — structural geographic difference between the two cases), Chapter 1 (Diamond axis argument extended to mountain ranges)

## Content

**Same mountain type. Perpendicular orientations. Opposite civilizational consequences.**

**Tian Shan (east-west):** Runs parallel to the Silk Road trade routes. Provides snowmelt water while not blocking the corridor. Organizes traffic along its base. Generates chokepoint cities at every terrain transition (Kashgar, Turfan, Dunhuang). The mountain *enables* sustained east-west cultural exchange.

**Rockies (north-south):** Runs perpendicular to any potential east-west trade corridor. Blocks rather than organizes. Fragments east-west cultural diffusion. The most viable crossing (South Pass, Wyoming) never generated an equivalent urban node despite being the only viable east-west route for most of the range's length. No chokepoint city means insufficient sustained bidirectional traffic.

**The South Pass vs. Dunhuang test:**
- Both are terrain transition points
- Dunhuang became a city with monasteries, libraries, multilingual archives
- South Pass remained a geographic feature with no permanent settlement
- The traffic volume difference is the mountain orientation difference expressed in urban archaeology

**Diamond's axis argument extended.** Diamond argues that continental east-west orientation enables agricultural diffusion across similar latitudes. This extends the principle to mountain ranges: east-west mountains organize corridors, north-south mountains block them. The organizing logic applies at the feature level, not just the continental level.

**North American trade corridors ran north-south** — along both sides of the Rockies (Plains corridor east, California valley west). The Comanche Empire exploited the Plains corridor's north-south logic. Trade and diplomacy existed but clustered along these north-south corridors rather than crossing the east-west barrier.

## Why this matters

1. **Chapter 6 structural comparison.** The Eurasian steppe had the Tian Shan organizing a corridor. The American Plains had the Rockies blocking one. This is a geographic difference that explains why the Silk Road produced sustained exchange infrastructure and the American frontier did not — different mountain orientations, different corridor possibilities, different civilizational outcomes.

2. **South Pass vs. Dunhuang is a testable comparison.** Same function, different outcomes, explained by the framework. If Charlie asks "why didn't North America produce a Silk Road?" the answer is the mountain orientation.
