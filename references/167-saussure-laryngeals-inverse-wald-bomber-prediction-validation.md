## Calibration Update (added 2026-05-13) — Date Correction

The original card's "thirty-six years earlier" framing was wrong. The correct sequence:

- **1878** — Saussure publishes *Mémoire sur le système primitif des voyelles* (the work is dated 1879 on the title page but actually appeared December 1878), postulating "sonantic coefficients"
- **1915** — Bedřich Hrozný demonstrates that Hittite is Indo-European (this is the decipherment event, not the laryngeal confirmation)
- **1927** — Jerzy Kuryłowicz publishes "ə indo-européen et ḫ hittite," identifying the Hittite consonant ḫ as the reflex of Saussure's predicted laryngeals — *this* is the confirmation event

The actual gap is **~49 years** (1878 → 1927), not 36. Hrozný established Hittite as IE; Kuryłowicz applied it to laryngeal theory and substantiated Saussure's prediction. Only after Kuryłowicz's 1927 work did Indo-Europeanists generally accept the laryngeal theory.

**Framework impact: none.** The methodological exemplar still works. Saussure made a theoretical prediction; independent evidentiary instruments (Hittite decipherment, then specific laryngeal-pattern matching) eventually confirmed it. A 49-year gap is, if anything, a *stronger* case for the framework's "predictions can wait decades for confirmation" methodological claim than 36 years was. The two-step decipherment-then-application structure also better matches the framework's situation — pre-documentary predictions don't get confirmed by a single moment of discovery but through accumulated evidence over time.

## The Case

In 1878, the Swiss linguist Ferdinand de Saussure published *Mémoire sur le système primitif des voyelles dans les langues indo-européennes* — a theoretical analysis of the vowel system in Proto-Indo-European based purely on systematic comparison of daughter languages. Saussure noticed that the sound patterns in the documented daughter languages contained gaps that only made sense if certain now-vanished consonants had once existed in PIE. He called them "sonantic coefficients." He derived the prediction from the structure of the comparative evidence, without any direct evidence the sounds had ever existed in any extant or recovered language.

In 1915, the Czech linguist Bedřich Hrozný demonstrated that Hittite — a Bronze Age Anatolian language preserved in cuneiform tablets — was Indo-European. Hittite had branched off from PIE early enough to retain features all the later daughter languages had lost. Twelve years later, in 1927, the Polish linguist Jerzy Kuryłowicz published "ə indo-européen et ḫ hittite," identifying the Hittite consonant ḫ as the direct reflex of Saussure's predicted laryngeals. Forty-nine years after Saussure made the prediction, an independent evidentiary instrument confirmed it. Only after Kuryłowicz's 1927 paper did Indo-Europeanists generally accept the laryngeal theory.

The prediction was falsifiable. The prediction was specific. The confirmation came from an independent evidentiary instrument operating decades after the prediction was made. This is the highest standard of scientific validation applied to historical linguistics.

## The Inverse Wald Bomber (Dave Stanton)

The conventional Wald survivorship-bias correction (card 064) operates by analyzing the planes that returned to find where the planes that didn't return must have been hit. The reasoning runs from existing pattern (damage distribution on returning aircraft) to inference about missing data (damage distribution on planes that didn't return).

The Saussure case is the inverse Wald bomber. The reasoning runs the same direction — from existing pattern to inference about missing data — but instead of stopping at inference, it makes a specific falsifiable prediction about what the missing data would look like if recovered. *If we could find a daughter language that branched off early enough, it would contain these specific consonants in these specific positions.* The prediction was on record. The wreckage was found forty years later exactly where the theory said it should be.

This is the analytical pattern card 162 named when it described historical linguistics as theoretical physics. Saussure's 1879 paper is the cleanest available exemplar of the pattern operating at full strength. The theory generated a prediction. The prediction was falsifiable. The evidence eventually arrived. The methodology was validated.

## Why This Matters for the Framework

Three reasons the Saussure case is the framework's most useful methodological exemplar:

1. **It is a famous, well-documented case from a respected academic field.** A reader skeptical of the framework's methodology can be pointed at the Saussure-Hittite validation as evidence that this style of inference produces durable knowledge. Historical linguistics is not generally regarded as speculative. The Saussure case is one of the reasons. The dissertation can borrow that credibility by demonstrating that its methodology operates in the same mode.

2. **It demonstrates that the time lag between prediction and confirmation is not an objection.** Saussure waited thirty-six years. The Hittite tablets had been sitting in archives that whole time. The prediction did not become less valid during the wait. The framework's predictive archaeology claims — that specific document caches should exist in specific locations, that specific patterns should be detectable in not-yet-excavated sites — operate under the same epistemic structure. The prediction is on record. The evidence may arrive in five years or fifty. The methodology is the same regardless.

3. **It shows the falsifiability standard the framework should aspire to.** Saussure's prediction was specific enough that Hittite either contained the laryngeals or it didn't. The framework's predictions should be specific enough that future evidence either confirms or refutes them. Vague predictions that can be made compatible with any outcome are not validation — they are insulation. The framework benefits from making predictions specific enough that confirmation or refutation is possible.

## What This Implies for the Framework

Three additions to the framework's apparatus:

1. **The framework can point at a specific historical exemplar when defending its methodology.** Saussure 1879 → Hittite 1915 is the framework's clearest comparable case for the methodological claim that theoretical prediction can produce durable historical knowledge confirmed by independent evidentiary instruments operating decades later. A skeptical reader can be directed to this case as the model the framework operates on.

2. **The framework's predictions should be calibrated to Saussure's standard of specificity.** Not vague claims that can be retrofit to any outcome. Specific predictions about what evidence should exist and what it should look like, such that confirmation or refutation is possible. The dissertation's existing predictions can be audited against this standard, and future predictions can be drafted to meet it.

3. **The time lag is part of the methodology, not a defect.** Predictions made under conditions of evidence scarcity may wait decades or longer for confirmation. The framework can be candid that its predictions operate on this timescale. The wait does not invalidate the methodology — it is the methodology working as designed, in a field where the relevant evidence does not arrive on demand.
