## Calibration Note

The framework prediction aligns with one of the documented explanations in current Chinese-historiography consensus. Three calibrations baked in:

1. **Number of moves: five per the canonical source.** *Records of the Grand Historian* (Sima Qian's *Shiji*) records **five Shang capital moves** before Pan Geng's final move to Yin (~1300 BCE). Hammond may have given a different count (Dave's voice-to-text rendered it as "nine"); the variance could reflect different sources, counting of sub-moves, or pre-Shang movements included. The framework's structural claim works at any count of multiple moves followed by stabilization; verifying the specific Hammond number is a future-pass task.

2. **Multi-factor causation is the consensus position.** The hydraulic-flooding-instability theory is *one* of several documented explanations for Shang capital moves, alongside military pressure from northern groups, internal political instability and elite conflict, resource depletion, and ritual/religious factors. Most current scholarship argues multi-factor causation. The framework's distinctive contribution is not replacing conventional explanations with the hydraulic one but **integrating the hydraulic factor with the Mandate-of-Heaven legitimation mechanism (card 209)** to show why hydraulic considerations are structurally privileged over other relocation drivers in the specific Shang case.

3. **Yin stabilization mechanism is genuinely uncertain.** The ~250-year stability at Yin after Pan Geng's move could reflect improved hydraulic engineering, climate stabilization, political consolidation, or some combination. The framework should claim the structural pattern (prior instability tracked a moving optimum; subsequent stability ended administrative migration) without staking confidence on which specific factor produced the stability.

## The Framework Move

The framework's apparatus has been built primarily around terrain features stable across millennia — rivers, mountains, gravity wells, chokepoints, friction sieves (card 208 toolkit). The Shang capital-migration case adds a discrete new category to the framework: **administrative response to terrain dynamism — where the terrain itself is dynamic, the political-administrative center adapts to track the moving optimum.**

In the Shang case, the Yellow River was genuinely dynamic in this period. Geological archives show frequent extreme flood occurrences between approximately 3200-3000 cal. yr BP (c. 1200-1000 BCE, matching Late Shang). The loess plateau deposition meant the riverbed was constantly shifting; flooding patterns changed; the Goldilocks zone of reliable but manageable water access moved with the river itself.

The Mandate-of-Heaven hydraulic-legitimation mechanism (card 209) makes following the water structurally privileged over other locational factors. The legitimacy of the Shang ruler was hydraulically defined: control the water, demonstrate the Mandate of Heaven; fail to control floods, forfeit the Mandate. If the water moves, the power center should move with it — not only because relocating is practical but because **the ruler's legitimacy is conditional on co-location with the hydraulic optimum**. The same factor that produces capital migration (water relocation) also produces the legitimating requirement to migrate (Mandate conditional on hydraulic control).

**Painting the bullseye around the arrow** is the framework's vocabulary for this mechanism. Rather than attempting to control a river that keeps changing course (which Bronze Age hydraulic engineering could not do at scale), move the administrative center to wherever the river currently offers the best terms. The capital tracks the optimum rather than the optimum being engineered to suit the capital. The Mandate-of-Heaven framework makes this not just adaptive but doctrinally proper.

## The Yin Stabilization

Pan Geng's move to Yin (~1300 BCE, modern Anyang) became the stable capital for the dynasty's last ~250 years. The mechanism is uncertain but three candidate explanations operate within the framework's apparatus:

- **Hydraulic engineering improvement.** Late Shang technology produced sufficient irrigation and flood-control infrastructure to make a fixed location manageable regardless of river variation.
- **Climate stabilization.** A period of relative river stability made fixed-capital viable for the first time, eliminating the moving-optimum problem.
- **Political consolidation.** Pan Geng's reform consolidated elite power sufficiently to override factional pressures that had driven prior moves, and the hydraulic factor became secondary to political stability.

The candidate explanations are not mutually exclusive. All three could compound. The framework's stable claim is the structural pattern: prior instability tracked a moving optimum, and subsequent stability ended administrative migration. The specific stability mechanism is a question for empirical investigation, not framework prediction.

## Cross-Civilization Recurrence

The administrative-response-to-terrain-dynamism mechanism the framework names in the Shang case should recur wherever:

1. The terrain feature organizing the civilization is dynamic on relevant timescales
2. The legitimating framework ties political authority to managing that feature
3. The civilization's technology cannot stabilize the terrain itself

Candidate parallel cases:

- **Egyptian capital shifts** (Memphis → Thebes → Amarna → Tanis → Alexandria) — the Nile delta is dynamic at slower timescales than the Yellow River, but documented capital relocations correlate with delta shifts and political-religious reorganizations that tracked Nile management
- **Mesopotamian city succession** (Ur → Akkad → Babylon → Nineveh → Ctesiphon → Baghdad) — Tigris-Euphrates course changes documented across the documentary period correlate with successive city-rises and city-abandonments along the rivers; the major hydraulic-management cities migrated as the rivers migrated
- **Indus Valley abandonments** (Harappa, Mohenjo-daro) — the Indus and Saraswati (Ghaggar-Hakra) river shifts in the late 3rd millennium BCE are documented, and the major urban centers were abandoned in the same period; the framework's prediction would be that the population followed the rivers to new sites that subsequent archaeology has only partially mapped
- **Contemporary financial-capital migration** (Boston → Philadelphia → New York → ?) — a non-hydraulic case where the relevant "terrain" is the financial network's center-of-gravity rather than literal terrain; the same structural mechanism (administrative center tracks moving optimum) operates with different specific content

The Shang case is the framework's clearest pre-documentary case because the legitimating framework (Mandate of Heaven), the dynamic terrain (Yellow River), and the documented migrations (five canonical moves plus Pan Geng's stabilization at Yin) are all empirically anchored.
