# Chapter 6 Needs Hansen's Eastern Perspective — Avoiding Sender-Side Encoding

**Source:** Alpha discussion, 2026-04-23 (Dave's pushback on Alpha's dismissal)
**Type:** connection
**Relevant to:** Chapter 6 (Parallel Conquests — structural requirement)

## Content

**The risk:** Chapter 6 was conceived primarily from Tasar's seminar (semester 1) — the Russian military push, Kenesary's rebellion, the railroad, the cotton monoculture. That's the western/Russian perspective. Without the eastern view, the chapter tells the conquest from the conqueror's vantage — exactly the sender-side encoding the book argues against.

**Hansen provides the receiver-side / eastern view:**
- Chinese power receding from the Tarim Basin — the vacuum that made Russian expansion possible
- Nomadic disruption filling the vacuum as sedentary pressure dropped (the steppe asserting itself, as always)
- Trade disruption reducing cotton supply — the economic gradient that pulled Russia south
- The Silk Road corridor going commercially dark just as Russia arrived to exploit it
- The corridor consumed from both ends simultaneously: Russia pushing east/south, Britain pushing into India/Afghanistan, China too weak to hold its western frontier

**Without Hansen, Chapter 6 risks:**
1. Telling the Russian story from Russian sources (Morrison) — sender-side
2. Telling the American story from American sources (Hämäläinen, White) — sender-side
3. Missing why the vacuum existed in the first place — the eastern conditions that made the conquest possible
4. Treating the conquests as purely western phenomena rather than as responses to a simultaneous eastern recession

**With Hansen, Chapter 6 gains:**
- The reason the corridor was available for conquest (Chinese recession + nomadic disruption)
- The economic logic (cotton supply disruption creating the gradient)
- The confirmation that the same moment looks different from each end of the corridor
- The receiver-side of the conquest — what the corridor was experiencing as Russia arrived

## Why this matters

The book's entire methodology is receiver-side analysis. Chapter 6 — the flagship evidence chapter — cannot be sender-side encoded. It must show the conquest from the conquered corridor's perspective as well as the conquering empires' perspectives. Hansen's eastern view is structurally necessary, not supplementary.

**Process note:** Alpha initially dismissed Hansen's convergence with Tasar as "not a new card — the stubs already have this." Dave correctly pushed back: without the eastern perspective explicitly incorporated, the chapter risks the same sender-side encoding the book critiques in everyone else.
