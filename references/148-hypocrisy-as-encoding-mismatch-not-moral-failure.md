# Hypocrisy as Encoding Mismatch — A Methodological Note

**Source:** Beta session synthesis (Dave Stanton observation), 2026-05-09
**Type:** methodology / framework extension
**Relevant to:** Chapter 1 (the framework / sender-receiver encoding), Chapter 6 (Parallel Conquests), Chapter 7 (Great Man's Hardest Test), ref 053 (tributary system as sender-receiver encoding), ref 140 (reminiscence bump reframe), ref 146 (Hawaii encoding work), ref 147 (1890s imperial moment)

## The Move

The word *hypocrisy* implies a conscious gap between stated values and actual behavior — a unified self with consistent access to its own motivations who then deliberately chooses to act against them. The concept assumes a single coherent frame that the person is betraying.

Most of what looks like hypocrisy in historical actors and contemporary observers is something structurally different: multiple frames running simultaneously, encoded at different times under different conditions, never reconciled because the conditions never required reconciliation. Each frame is locally coherent. The contradictions are visible only from outside the encoding.

Reframing apparent hypocrisy as encoding mismatch is not exculpation. It does not excuse the behavior. It makes the behavior legible in a way that pure moral condemnation cannot. That legibility is the precondition for changing anything — you cannot shame a structural encoding mismatch into resolution; you have to change the frame.

## Historical Applications

**The Comanche raiding Mexican settlements while trading with New Mexican settlements** is not hypocrisy. It is coherent behavior within a frame that treated fixed sedentary settlements as raid targets and mobile commercial partners as trading relationships. The apparent contradiction only exists if a sedentary diplomatic frame is imposed that expects unified consistent treatment of all Spanish-descended people. The Comanche frame did not include that requirement. (See cards 110, 121, 137 for the full nomadic-sedentary interface logic.)

**The 1890s American expansionist** genuinely believed in liberty and self-determination AND genuinely believed Hawaii required American civilization AND genuinely believed Japanese competition required strategic response. None of those beliefs felt contradictory from inside the frame. The hypocrisy is visible only from outside the encoding — and only after later events made the contradiction politically salient. (See cards 146, 147.)

**Spain treating Comanche raiding as barbarism while extracting silver from New World mines through encomienda labor** is not hypocrisy. Each frame was locally coherent within the institutional vocabulary that produced it. The mining operation was framed as Crown legitimacy and Christian conversion. The Comanche raiding was framed as savage disruption of Christian order. Neither frame had to engage the other.

**The British administrator drawing Sykes-Picot borders while genuinely believing in self-determination** is not hypocrisy. Self-determination as a principle and strategic border-drawing as a practice operated in different frames addressing different audiences. They never had to reconcile because the institutional structure that would have forced reconciliation did not exist. (See ref 117.)

In every case, the standard moral-failure reading (*they were hypocrites*) generates less analytical traction than the encoding reading (*they were running incompatible frames that never had to integrate*). The encoding reading explains why the behavior was possible. The hypocrisy reading explains only that the behavior was wrong.

## The Methodological Move

Throughout the dissertation, the move is the same. Read the historical actor's behavior as coherent within their encoding. Identify what frames were available to them and which one they were running for which audience. Note where the frames came from and what conditions they were calibrated to. Read the contradiction as a structural feature of having multiple frames, not as a moral feature of having one frame the actor is betraying.

This is not a request for sympathy with destructive actors. The destruction is real. The damage is real. The lives are real. But moral condemnation alone cannot answer the question of *how the behavior was structurally possible* — and without answering that question, no preventative analysis is possible. Calling Cortés a hypocrite or the railroad executives indifferent monsters explains nothing about how to prevent the next Cortés or the next railroad. Reading the encoding mismatch explains where the leverage actually sits.

## Why This Matters Beyond Historical Analysis

The same move applies to contemporary observation. Most of what looks like hypocrisy in living people — institutional, political, personal — is multiple frames running simultaneously without reconciliation. The frames feel locally coherent because each was encoded under conditions that did not include the other. The shame-the-hypocrite move is satisfying but ineffective; it asks the actor to repair a unity that was never there. The change-the-frame move is harder but more productive; it asks the conditions to be altered such that the frames would have to integrate.

You can shame hypocrisy. You cannot shame a structural encoding mismatch. You have to change the frame.
