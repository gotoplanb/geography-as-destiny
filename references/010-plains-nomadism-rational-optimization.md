# Plains Nomadism as Rational Geographic Optimization

**Source:** Beta seminar discussion, 2026-04-12
**Type:** connection
**Relevant to:** Chapter 1 (The Distribution), Chapter 6 (Parallel Conquests), engages Diamond directly

## Content

The Great Plains didn't fail to produce sedentary civilization. Sedentary civilization would have been an irrational choice given the geography.

**The argument:**
- The Plains are extraordinarily fertile — some of the best topsoil on earth. The lack of settlement isn't resource poverty.
- The buffalo economy was so productive that sedentary agriculture had no competitive advantage. Why clear land, build irrigation, store grain, and defend a fixed location when you can follow herds that self-replicate and self-transport?
- North America's continental orientation enabled north-south seasonal migration. You track abundance latitudinally without needing to store surplus. Granaries solve the problem of seasonal scarcity at a fixed location. If you can move to where the food is, you don't need the solution.

**The Diamond inversion:**
- Diamond's axis argument: Eurasia's east-west orientation enabled the spread of agriculture across similar latitudes. North-south oriented continents made diffusion harder.
- The inversion: North America's north-south orientation didn't just make agriculture harder to *spread* — it made agriculture harder to *justify*. You can migrate seasonally without hitting a sea or a mountain range that forces you to stop and store. Eurasia's east-west axis means you run out of continent before you run out of seasons. You stop. You store. You settle. That's the origin of sedentary civilization.
- Same geographic variable (continental axis), same analytical framework (Diamond's), opposite conclusion depending on what the terrain actually rewards. This is the probabilistic frame: geography doesn't determine "agriculture or not." It shifts the probability distribution of which strategy is rational.

**The buffalo destruction as economic warfare:**
- The railroad didn't just bring settlers. It brought the systematic extermination of the buffalo — the entire economic foundation of the Plains optimization. Kill the buffalo and the rational basis for mobile life collapses completely.
- This was deliberate policy, not an accident. The empire understood (at least intuitively) that the geographic optimization had to be destroyed before the people could be conquered. You can't just defeat the nomads militarily — you have to destroy the *geographic logic* that makes their lifestyle viable.
- Parallel: Russia's imposition of cotton monoculture in Central Asia destroyed the pastoral economy the same way. Different crop, same structural move — replace the geographic optimization with an extractive one that requires sedentary infrastructure the state can control.

## Why this matters

This is one of the most important conceptual moves in the book. It takes Diamond's strongest argument (continental axis) and shows that it cuts both ways — the same variable that explains why Eurasia developed agriculture also explains why the Plains rationally *didn't*. That's not a refutation of Diamond. It's a demonstration that the probabilistic frame is more powerful than his binary one.

It also reframes the conquest: the railroad + buffalo destruction isn't just military victory. It's the deliberate destruction of a rational geographic optimization and its replacement with a different one. The empire doesn't just conquer the space — it rewrites the space's economic logic.
