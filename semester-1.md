## Reading List

### 1. Alexander Morrison — *The Russian Conquest of Central Asia* (2021)
The primary text. The definitive modern English-language account of the conquest. Read alongside Tasar — Morrison gives you the archive where Tasar gives you the lecture. His revisionist argument (the conquest was ad hoc, not a grand strategy) strengthens the geographic-determinism thesis: if even the individuals were improvising, the structural outcome was terrain-driven.

**Focus while reading:** The Transcaspian Railway and how it changed the military calculus. The logistics of projecting state power across the steppe. The gap between St. Petersburg's intentions and what local commanders actually did.

### 2. S. Frederick Starr — *Lost Enlightenment* (2013)
What was conquered. The Central Asian golden age — al-Khwarizmi, Ibn Sina, Ulugh Beg. You can't write the conquest chapter without knowing what the railroad rolled over, and you can't write the Timurid chapter without this foundation.

**Focus while reading:** The geography of Transoxiana — why *this specific terrain* between the Oxus and Syr Darya produced this concentration of intellectual output. The decline after the Mongols — was it geographically conditioned?

### 3. Pekka Hämäläinen — *The Comanche Empire* (2008)
The parallel. Read at the same time as Morrison. The Comanche built an empire on the same geographic logic the Central Asian nomads used — horse-based mobility across open terrain. The same technology (railroad + firearms) ended both. The parallels will surface naturally if the books are side by side.

**Focus while reading:** The Comanchería as a geographic system — terrain, horse, trade network. The collapse: how fast did it happen once the railroad arrived? The destruction of the buffalo herds as the economic equivalent of Russia's cotton monoculture.
