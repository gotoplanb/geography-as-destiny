# Chapter 1: The Distribution
### *Geography as probability, not destiny*

**Status:** Stub — sources and argument scaffolding

---

## Argument Structure

This chapter does five things:
1. Establishes the probabilistic frame — geography sets distributions, not outcomes
2. Introduces the three lenses and shows why you need all three
3. Presents the "replication test" that breaks great man theory: similar geographic contexts produce similar outcomes independently, across cultures with no contact
4. Shows that geographic convergence goes deeper than institutions — it shapes epistemology itself (divination systems as geographically-determined pattern recognition; see `references/001-divination-as-geographic-epistemology.md`)
5. Lays out the methodological toolkit — the scientific controls we borrow and the ones we can't use (see `references/003-falsifiability-and-counterfactual-method.md`):
   - **Falsifiability:** the framework must name conditions under which it fails, or it's a belief, not a theory. Cases where geography *doesn't* predict the outcome are features, not bugs.
   - **Counterfactual removal test:** for any great man claim, remove the person and ask if the structural outcome changes. For any geographic claim, change the terrain and ask the same.
   - **Probabilistic language:** distributions, not determinism. We can't generate actual curves with real data (sample size = 1, history happened once), but the discipline of thinking in likelihoods produces better history than narrative assertion without methodological controls.
   - **Parallel cases as natural experiments:** not clean, but convergence across independent cases (Russia/America, Nile/Yellow River) is more than anecdote and less than proof. It's evidence at the level history can provide.

The chapter ends by previewing the Parallel Conquests as the thesis demonstrated at scale.

## Source Stubs

### The Geographic Determinism Foundation

**Jared Diamond — *Guns, Germs, and Steel* (1997)**
The starting point, not the destination. Diamond's argument that continental axis orientation (east-west vs. north-south) determined the spread of agriculture and domesticated animals is the macro-scale foundation this book extends. His weakness is that he stays almost entirely at the millennia scale and treats geography as binary (you have it or you don't) rather than probabilistic (it shifts the likelihood). We need him for the framework's floor. We depart from him on resolution and on the friction-collapse dynamic he doesn't address.

**Ian Morris — *Why the West Rules — For Now* (2010)**
Morris builds a quantitative "social development index" and tracks East vs. West over 15,000 years. His core claim: "maps, not chaps" — geography explains the broad pattern, individual leaders explain the noise. This is closer to our probabilistic frame than Diamond. Morris also explicitly addresses why geography's advantage shifts over time (what he calls "the paradox of development"), which connects directly to friction collapse. His index methodology is debatable but the ambition to quantify geographic advantage is exactly right.

**Fernand Braudel — *The Mediterranean and the Mediterranean World in the Age of Philip II* (1949)**
Braudel invented the idea of geographic time vs. human time — the *longue durée*. His Mediterranean is the prototype for treating a geographic space as the protagonist rather than any king or empire. His three-tier model (geographic time / social time / event time) maps loosely onto our three lenses. He's the intellectual ancestor of everything we're doing, and the chapter should acknowledge that lineage. Dense reading but the first 200 pages on the physical Mediterranean are extraordinary.

**Alfred Crosby — *Ecological Imperialism: The Biological Expansion of Europe* (2004, 2nd ed.)**
Crosby extends Diamond's logic into the colonial period — European expansion succeeded in "Neo-Europes" (temperate zones with similar ecology) and stalled in the tropics. This is geographic determinism applied to a specific historical wave, and it's useful for showing that the distribution doesn't just operate at the civilizational-origins scale. It operates every time a population encounters new terrain. Relevant to the Parallel Conquests chapter too — both the American West and Central Asian steppe were "Neo-Europes" in Crosby's sense.

### The Friction Collapse / Technology Shock Lens

**Carlota Perez — *Technological Revolutions and Financial Capital* (2002)**
Perez models technology as arriving in "great surges" that reshape the entire economic and institutional landscape before a new equilibrium settles. Her framework is economic, not geographic, but the structural pattern — long stability, sudden disruption, new equilibrium — is exactly the punctuated equilibrium we're describing. She gives us the vocabulary for how friction collapses actually propagate through systems. The installation/deployment distinction she draws maps onto Rogers' early-adopter/late-majority phases.

**William McNeill — *The Pursuit of Power: Technology, Armed Force, and Society since A.D. 1000* (1982)**
McNeill tracks how military technology reshapes the relationship between geography and political power. The gunpowder revolution, the railroad, the telegraph — each one changes what terrain is defensible and what isn't. This is friction collapse applied specifically to military geography, which is the core of the Parallel Conquests argument. McNeill is the source that most directly connects technology shocks to geographic redistribution of power.

**Vaclav Smil — *Energy and Civilization: A History* (2017)**
Smil's argument is that energy transitions are the deepest driver of civilizational change. Each energy regime (biomass → fossil fuels → electricity) collapses a different kind of friction. The railroad is a fossil-fuel story. The internet is an electricity story. Smil gives us the physical substrate underneath our friction-collapse argument — it's not abstract "technology" that resets distributions, it's specifically the ability to deploy energy in new ways across terrain.

### The Diffusion of Innovations Lens

**Everett Rogers — *Diffusion of Innovations* (5th ed., 2003)**
The foundational text. Rogers' S-curve model, adopter categories (innovators → early adopters → early majority → late majority → laggards), and the factors that determine adoption rate (relative advantage, compatibility, complexity, trialability, observability) are the analytical toolkit we apply throughout the book. The chapter needs to introduce Rogers' framework clearly enough that it can be deployed in every subsequent chapter without re-explanation. Key point: Rogers developed this empirically, from agricultural extension studies, not from theory. The statistical texture is baked in.

**Peter Turchin — *Historical Dynamics: Why States Rise and Fall* (2003)**
Turchin applies mathematical modeling to historical cycles — he calls it "cliodynamics." His work on the spread of imperial frontiers and the demographic-structural theory of state collapse gives us a quantitative vocabulary for diffusion at the civilizational scale. Controversial in traditional history departments but methodologically aligned with our probabilistic frame. His concept of "meta-ethnic frontiers" (where the pressure of living next to a very different culture produces intense group solidarity) is directly relevant to the steppe/settled boundary.

**James C. Scott — *The Art of Not Being Governed* (2009)**
Scott's argument about "Zomia" — the highland massif of Southeast Asia as a region where people deliberately evaded state incorporation — is the anti-diffusion case. Not everyone adopts. Some populations actively resist. Scott gives us the laggard category with political content: non-adoption as a rational strategy, not as backwardness. This is essential for intellectual honesty — the diffusion frame needs the counter-case built in.

### The Anti-Great-Man Statistical Argument

**Peter Turchin — *Ultrasociety: How 10,000 Years of War Made Humans the Greatest Cooperators on Earth* (2016)**
Turchin's data on the independent emergence of similar political structures in similar geographic contexts is the statistical backbone of the anti-great-man argument. If outcomes depended primarily on individuals, you wouldn't get convergent evolution of state forms. But you do — river valley civilizations converge on bureaucratic states, steppe peoples converge on confederacies, maritime peoples converge on merchant republics. Turchin quantifies what Diamond asserts.

**Joseph Henrich — *The WEIRDest People in the World* (2020)**
Henrich traces how the Catholic Church's marriage policies accidentally dissolved European kin networks and produced the individualist, institution-trusting psychology that enabled Western state formation. The relevance: this is a case study in how a cultural innovation (marriage rules) interacts with geographic context (fragmented European terrain) to shift the distribution over centuries. Henrich is careful about causality and gives us a model for how to discuss culture without falling into either determinism or "anything goes."

## Narrative Descent Candidates

The TOC suggests the view from the Pamir Knot. Still strong — standing at the hinge of Eurasia where every direction leads to a different civilization, one point of terrain that explains more than ten kings. But alternatives worth considering:

- **A farmer in two river valleys** — the same life, same administrative structures, different continent, no contact. The Nile and the Yellow River, same century. The visual parallel *is* the argument.
- **Two merchants who never meet** — one in Samarkand, one in Venice, both operating chokepoint economies shaped by identical geographic logic.
